Question 1 — Unit Conversion

Converting between metric units using dimensional analysis.

Question 1

How many centimeters are there in 32.00 nm?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify the conversion chain: nm to m, then m to cm.

1 nm = 10-9 m and 1 m = 100 cm = 102 cm

Step 2 — Set up dimensional analysis.

\[ 32.00 \text{ nm} \times \frac{10^{-9} \text{ m}}{1 \text{ nm}} \times \frac{100 \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ m}} \]

Step 3 — Combine the powers of ten.

\(32.00 \times 10^{-9} \times 10^{2} = 32.00 \times 10^{-7}\)

Answer: \(3.200 \times 10^{-6}\) cm

Question 2 — Weighted Average Atomic Mass

The weighted average atomic mass accounts for both the mass and natural abundance of each isotope.

Question 2

An element has the following isotope masses and percent abundances. Calculate the weighted average atomic mass.

Isotope Mass (amu)Percent Abundance
27.97692792.2297%
28.9764954.6832%
29.9737703.0872%
Walkthrough

Step 1 — Convert each percent abundance to a decimal fraction.

92.2297% = 0.922297    4.6832% = 0.046832    3.0872% = 0.030872

Step 2 — Multiply each isotope mass by its decimal abundance.

\[ \bar{m} = (27.976927)(0.922297) + (28.976495)(0.046832) + (29.973770)(0.030872) \]

\(= 25.8030 + 1.3570 + 0.9254\)

Step 3 — Sum the contributions.

\(25.8030 + 1.3570 + 0.9254 = 28.0854\)

Weighted average atomic mass: 28.09 amu (Silicon)

Question 3 — Mole and Avogadro Calculations

Use molar mass and Avogadro's number (\(N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}\) atoms/mol) to convert between grams and atoms.

Question 3

How many atoms of oxygen are there in 25.5 g of carbon dioxide?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find the molar mass of CO2.

\(M(\text{CO}_2) = 12.011 + 2(15.999) = 44.009 \text{ g/mol}\)

Step 2 — Convert grams to moles of CO2.

\[ n(\text{CO}_2) = \frac{25.5 \text{ g}}{44.009 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.5794 \text{ mol} \]

Step 3 — Determine moles of oxygen atoms.

Each CO2 molecule contains 2 oxygen atoms.

\(n(\text{O}) = 0.5794 \times 2 = 1.1588 \text{ mol O atoms}\)

Step 4 — Convert moles to atoms using Avogadro's number.

\[ N(\text{O}) = 1.1588 \text{ mol} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \frac{\text{atoms}}{\text{mol}} \]

Answer: \(6.98 \times 10^{23}\) oxygen atoms

Question 4 — Empirical and Molecular Formula

Find the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (empirical formula), then use the molar mass to find the molecular formula.

Question 4

A compound was found to contain 47.37% carbon, 10.59% hydrogen, and 42.04% oxygen by mass. What is the empirical formula? If the molecular formula molar mass is 228.286 g/mol, what is the molecular formula?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Assume 100 g sample; each percent becomes grams.

C: 47.37 g    H: 10.59 g    O: 42.04 g

Step 2 — Convert grams to moles for each element.

\[ n(\text{C}) = \frac{47.37}{12.011} = 3.9440 \text{ mol} \quad n(\text{H}) = \frac{10.59}{1.008} = 10.506 \text{ mol} \quad n(\text{O}) = \frac{42.04}{15.999} = 2.6276 \text{ mol} \]

Step 3 — Divide by the smallest value (2.6276) to find mole ratios.

\(\text{C}: \frac{3.9440}{2.6276} = 1.501 \quad \text{H}: \frac{10.506}{2.6276} = 3.998 \quad \text{O}: \frac{2.6276}{2.6276} = 1.000\)

Step 4 — The ratio 1.5 : 4 : 1 is not whole numbers; multiply by 2.

C: 3    H: 8    O: 2

Empirical formula: C3H8O2

Step 5 — Find the empirical formula molar mass.

\(M(\text{EF}) = 3(12.011) + 8(1.008) + 2(15.999) = 36.033 + 8.064 + 31.998 = 76.095 \text{ g/mol}\)

Step 6 — Find n, the multiplier.

\[ n = \frac{228.286}{76.095} = 3.00 \]

Step 7 — Multiply the empirical formula by n = 3.

Empirical formula: C3H8O2   |   Molecular formula: C9H24O6

Question 5 — Solution Concentration

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / liters of solution. Account for ion dissociation when finding ion concentrations.

Question 5

Assume you dissolve 6.73 g sodium carbonate in enough water to make 275 mL of solution. What is the concentration of the sodium ion?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find the molar mass of sodium carbonate (Na2CO3).

\(M(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3) = 2(22.990) + 12.011 + 3(15.999) = 45.980 + 12.011 + 47.997 = 105.988 \text{ g/mol}\)

Step 2 — Convert grams to moles of Na2CO3.

\[ n(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3) = \frac{6.73 \text{ g}}{105.988 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.06350 \text{ mol} \]

Step 3 — Determine moles of Na+ ions.

Na2CO3 dissociates completely: Na2CO3 → 2 Na+ + CO32-

\(n(\text{Na}^+) = 2 \times 0.06350 = 0.12700 \text{ mol}\)

Step 4 — Convert volume to liters and calculate molarity.

275 mL = 0.275 L

\[ [\text{Na}^+] = \frac{0.12700 \text{ mol}}{0.275 \text{ L}} = 0.4618 \text{ M} \]

Answer: [Na+] = 0.4618 M

Question 6 — Chemical Nomenclature

Naming ionic compounds, covalent compounds, acids, and writing formulas from names.

Question 6

Give the name or formula for each of the following compounds:

  1. Carbonic acid
  2. Lead(IV) sulfite
  3. Magnesium hydroxide
  4. Aluminum sulfide
  5. Cu3(PO4)2
  6. KCH3CO2
  7. N2O4
  8. H2S
Walkthrough

A. Carbonic acid — Write the formula.

Carbonic acid is a common acid formed when CO2 dissolves in water.

Formula: H2CO3

B. Lead(IV) sulfite — Write the formula.

Lead(IV) means Pb with a +4 charge. Sulfite is SO32-.

To balance: 4+ charge needs two sulfite ions (each 2-). Formula: Pb(SO3)2

C. Magnesium hydroxide — Write the formula.

Magnesium is Mg2+. Hydroxide is OH-. Two OH- balance one Mg2+.

Formula: Mg(OH)2

D. Aluminum sulfide — Write the formula.

Aluminum is Al3+. Sulfide is S2-. Use cross-multiply method: Al2S3.

Formula: Al2S3

E. Cu3(PO4)2 — Give the name.

Phosphate is PO43-; two phosphate = 6- total charge. Three copper ions share 6+, so each Cu is +2.

Name: Copper(II) phosphate

F. KCH3CO2 — Give the name.

K+ is potassium. CH3CO2- is the acetate ion.

Name: Potassium acetate

G. N2O4 — Give the name.

Covalent compound named with Greek prefixes. di- for 2 nitrogen, tetra- for 4 oxygen.

Name: Dinitrogen tetroxide

H. H2S — Give the name.

Binary acid in aqueous solution: hydro- prefix + sulfur root + -ic acid.

Name: Hydrosulfuric acid (or hydrogen sulfide as a pure gas)
