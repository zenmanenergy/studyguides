AP US History — Unit 5: Period 5 (1844–1877)

Manifest Destiny and the Mexican-American War

  • Polk elected 1844 on expansionist platform; believed US destined to stretch to Pacific
  • Mexican-American War (1846–48): disputed Texas border; US won decisively
  • Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848): Mexico ceded California, New Mexico, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, Colorado
  • Wilmot Proviso (1846): proposed banning slavery in new territories; passed House, failed Senate; intensified sectional debate
  • California Gold Rush (1848–49) brought 300,000 migrants; California applied as free state → crisis

The Civil War (1861–1865)

  • Lincoln's election (1860) triggered secession; 11 Southern states formed Confederate States of America
  • Fort Sumter attack (April 1861) began the war
  • Key turning points: Antietam (Sept 1862) — bloodiest single day; enabled Emancipation Proclamation; Gettysburg (July 1863) — Confederate invasion repelled; Vicksburg (July 1863) — Union controls Mississippi
  • Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1, 1863): freed enslaved people in Confederate states; reframed war as fight against slavery; allowed Black men to enlist (180,000 served)
  • Total war strategy: Sherman's March to the Sea (1864) destroyed Confederate infrastructure and morale
  • Appomattox, April 9, 1865: Lee surrendered to Grant; Lincoln assassinated 5 days later

AmendmentYearContent

  • Freedmen's Bureau: provided food, education, labor contracts to formerly enslaved people
  • Black men voted, held office; Hiram Revels and Blanche Bruce in Senate
  • Black Codes and KKK: Southern resistance to Black freedom; terrorism and legal restrictions
  • Compromise of 1877: Hayes became president; federal troops withdrawn from South; Reconstruction ended; "Redeemer" Democrats took back Southern governments
  • Sharecropping system kept formerly enslaved people in debt servitude
