Question 1 — Unit Conversion

Converting between metric units using dimensional analysis.

Question 1

How many centimeters are there in 32.00 nm?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify the conversion chain: nm to m, then m to cm.

1 nm = 10-9 m and 1 m = 100 cm = 102 cm

Step 2 — Set up dimensional analysis.

\[ 32.00 \text{ nm} \times \frac{10^{-9} \text{ m}}{1 \text{ nm}} \times \frac{100 \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ m}} \]

Step 3 — Combine the powers of ten.

\(32.00 \times 10^{-9} \times 10^{2} = 32.00 \times 10^{-7}\)

Answer: \(3.200 \times 10^{-6}\) cm

Question 2 — Weighted Average Atomic Mass

The weighted average atomic mass accounts for both the mass and natural abundance of each isotope.

Question 2

An element has the following isotope masses and percent abundances. Calculate the weighted average atomic mass.

Isotope Mass (amu)Percent Abundance
27.97692792.2297%
28.9764954.6832%
29.9737703.0872%
Walkthrough

Step 1 — Convert each percent abundance to a decimal fraction.

92.2297% = 0.922297    4.6832% = 0.046832    3.0872% = 0.030872

Step 2 — Multiply each isotope mass by its decimal abundance.

\[ \bar{m} = (27.976927)(0.922297) + (28.976495)(0.046832) + (29.973770)(0.030872) \]

\(= 25.8030 + 1.3570 + 0.9254\)

Step 3 — Sum the contributions.

\(25.8030 + 1.3570 + 0.9254 = 28.0854\)

Weighted average atomic mass: 28.09 amu (Silicon)

Question 3 — Mole and Avogadro Calculations

Use molar mass and Avogadro's number (\(N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}\) atoms/mol) to convert between grams and atoms.

Question 3

How many atoms of oxygen are there in 25.5 g of carbon dioxide?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find the molar mass of CO2.

\(M(\text{CO}_2) = 12.011 + 2(15.999) = 44.009 \text{ g/mol}\)

Step 2 — Convert grams to moles of CO2.

\[ n(\text{CO}_2) = \frac{25.5 \text{ g}}{44.009 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.5794 \text{ mol} \]

Step 3 — Determine moles of oxygen atoms.

Each CO2 molecule contains 2 oxygen atoms.

\(n(\text{O}) = 0.5794 \times 2 = 1.1588 \text{ mol O atoms}\)

Step 4 — Convert moles to atoms using Avogadro's number.

\[ N(\text{O}) = 1.1588 \text{ mol} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \frac{\text{atoms}}{\text{mol}} \]

Answer: \(6.98 \times 10^{23}\) oxygen atoms

Question 4 — Empirical and Molecular Formula

Find the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (empirical formula), then use the molar mass to find the molecular formula.

Question 4

A compound was found to contain 47.37% carbon, 10.59% hydrogen, and 42.04% oxygen by mass. What is the empirical formula? If the molecular formula molar mass is 228.286 g/mol, what is the molecular formula?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Assume 100 g sample; each percent becomes grams.

C: 47.37 g    H: 10.59 g    O: 42.04 g

Step 2 — Convert grams to moles for each element.

\[ n(\text{C}) = \frac{47.37}{12.011} = 3.9440 \text{ mol} \quad n(\text{H}) = \frac{10.59}{1.008} = 10.506 \text{ mol} \quad n(\text{O}) = \frac{42.04}{15.999} = 2.6276 \text{ mol} \]

Step 3 — Divide by the smallest value (2.6276) to find mole ratios.

\(\text{C}: \frac{3.9440}{2.6276} = 1.501 \quad \text{H}: \frac{10.506}{2.6276} = 3.998 \quad \text{O}: \frac{2.6276}{2.6276} = 1.000\)

Step 4 — The ratio 1.5 : 4 : 1 is not whole numbers; multiply by 2.

C: 3    H: 8    O: 2

Empirical formula: C3H8O2

Step 5 — Find the empirical formula molar mass.

\(M(\text{EF}) = 3(12.011) + 8(1.008) + 2(15.999) = 36.033 + 8.064 + 31.998 = 76.095 \text{ g/mol}\)

Step 6 — Find n, the multiplier.

\[ n = \frac{228.286}{76.095} = 3.00 \]

Step 7 — Multiply the empirical formula by n = 3.

Empirical formula: C3H8O2   |   Molecular formula: C9H24O6

Question 5 — Solution Concentration

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / liters of solution. Account for ion dissociation when finding ion concentrations.

Question 5

Assume you dissolve 6.73 g sodium carbonate in enough water to make 275 mL of solution. What is the concentration of the sodium ion?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find the molar mass of sodium carbonate (Na2CO3).

\(M(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3) = 2(22.990) + 12.011 + 3(15.999) = 45.980 + 12.011 + 47.997 = 105.988 \text{ g/mol}\)

Step 2 — Convert grams to moles of Na2CO3.

\[ n(\text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3) = \frac{6.73 \text{ g}}{105.988 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.06350 \text{ mol} \]

Step 3 — Determine moles of Na+ ions.

Na2CO3 dissociates completely: Na2CO3 → 2 Na+ + CO32-

\(n(\text{Na}^+) = 2 \times 0.06350 = 0.12700 \text{ mol}\)

Step 4 — Convert volume to liters and calculate molarity.

275 mL = 0.275 L

\[ [\text{Na}^+] = \frac{0.12700 \text{ mol}}{0.275 \text{ L}} = 0.4618 \text{ M} \]

Answer: [Na+] = 0.4618 M

Question 6 — Chemical Nomenclature

Naming ionic compounds, covalent compounds, acids, and writing formulas from names.

Question 6

Give the name or formula for each of the following compounds:

  1. Carbonic acid
  2. Lead(IV) sulfite
  3. Magnesium hydroxide
  4. Aluminum sulfide
  5. Cu3(PO4)2
  6. KCH3CO2
  7. N2O4
  8. H2S
Walkthrough

A. Carbonic acid — Write the formula.

Carbonic acid is a common acid formed when CO2 dissolves in water.

Formula: H2CO3

B. Lead(IV) sulfite — Write the formula.

Lead(IV) means Pb with a +4 charge. Sulfite is SO32-.

To balance: 4+ charge needs two sulfite ions (each 2-). Formula: Pb(SO3)2

C. Magnesium hydroxide — Write the formula.

Magnesium is Mg2+. Hydroxide is OH-. Two OH- balance one Mg2+.

Formula: Mg(OH)2

D. Aluminum sulfide — Write the formula.

Aluminum is Al3+. Sulfide is S2-. Use cross-multiply method: Al2S3.

Formula: Al2S3

E. Cu3(PO4)2 — Give the name.

Phosphate is PO43-; two phosphate = 6- total charge. Three copper ions share 6+, so each Cu is +2.

Name: Copper(II) phosphate

F. KCH3CO2 — Give the name.

K+ is potassium. CH3CO2- is the acetate ion.

Name: Potassium acetate

G. N2O4 — Give the name.

Covalent compound named with Greek prefixes. di- for 2 nitrogen, tetra- for 4 oxygen.

Name: Dinitrogen tetroxide

H. H2S — Give the name.

Binary acid in aqueous solution: hydro- prefix + sulfur root + -ic acid.

Name: Hydrosulfuric acid (or hydrogen sulfide as a pure gas)

Question 7 — Hypothesis, Law, or Theory

Classify each statement as a hypothesis, a law, or a theory based on what it represents scientifically.

Question 7

Identify each of the following statements as being most similar to a hypothesis, a law, or a theory.

  1. The truck's gas mileage has dropped significantly; it's due for a tune-up.
  2. An object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a force.
  3. Earth's lithosphere is broken up into tectonic plates with boundaries determined by relative motion. Earthquakes, volcanic activity, mountain-building, and oceanic trench formation occur along the plate boundaries.
Walkthrough

Key definitions:

Hypothesis — A tentative, testable explanation of an observation; a starting point for investigation.

Law — A well-tested generalization that describes how nature consistently behaves (states what happens, not why).

Theory — A well-substantiated, falsifiable explanation that accounts for a broad range of observations (explains why).

(a) Truck gas mileage — Hypothesis

This is a tentative explanation for a single observation (poor mileage) that acts as a guide for investigation. It has not been tested yet and could be wrong.

(b) Object in motion — Law (Newton's First Law)

This is a concise, well-tested statement that describes what consistently happens. It does not explain why inertia exists — it only states the pattern.

(c) Tectonic plates — Theory (Plate Tectonic Theory)

This explains a broad range of phenomena (earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain-building) with a unifying mechanism. It is supported by vast evidence and is falsifiable.

PartClassification
(a) Truck tune-upHypothesis
(b) Object in motionLaw
(c) Tectonic platesTheory

Question 8 — Element, Compound, or Mixture

An element contains only one type of atom. A compound has a fixed ratio of two or more elements bonded together. A mixture combines substances that retain their individual properties.

Question 8

Classify each of the following as an element, a compound, or a mixture.

  1. Air
  2. Water
  3. Copper
  4. Sulfur
Walkthrough

(a) Air — Mixture

Air is a mixture of gases (mostly N2 and O2, with traces of Ar, CO2, etc.). The composition varies by location and is not fixed.

(b) Water — Compound

Water is H2O: two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom in a fixed ratio. It has a definite chemical formula and cannot be separated by physical means alone.

(c) Copper — Element

Copper (Cu) is a pure element. It contains only copper atoms and appears on the periodic table with atomic number 29.

(d) Sulfur — Element

Sulfur (S) is a pure element with atomic number 16. Though it often forms S8 rings, it is still a single element.

Question 9 — Pure Substances

A pure substance has a fixed, uniform chemical composition. Mixtures have variable compositions.

Question 9

Which of the following is a pure substance: water, pond water, or grapefruit juice? (Select all that apply.)

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Recall the definition of a pure substance.

A pure substance has a fixed chemical composition throughout. It is either a single element or a single compound.

Step 2 — Evaluate each option.

Water (H2O): Fixed formula, single compound → pure substance.

Pond water: Contains dissolved minerals, organisms, sediment, gases → variable composition → mixture.

Grapefruit juice: Contains water, sugars, acids, vitamins, pulp → variable composition → mixture.

Answer: Water is the only pure substance.

Question 10 — Homogeneous vs Heterogeneous Mixtures

A homogeneous mixture has a uniform composition throughout (e.g., saltwater). A heterogeneous mixture has visibly distinct regions or components.

Question 10

Identify each of the following as a homogeneous or heterogeneous mixture.

  1. A mixture of solid aluminum powder and solid iron(III) oxide particles
  2. Concrete
  3. A dilute solution of cobalt(II) sulfate in water
  4. Vinegar
Walkthrough

(a) Al powder + Fe2O3 particles — Heterogeneous

Two different solid powders mixed together. Individual particles are distinguishable — not uniform throughout.

(b) Concrete — Heterogeneous

Concrete contains cement, sand, gravel, and water. The composition is not uniform at the microscopic level; gravel and sand particles are visible.

(c) Dilute cobalt(II) sulfate solution — Homogeneous

CoSO4 fully dissolves in water, producing a uniform pink solution. The composition is the same throughout.

(d) Vinegar — Homogeneous

Vinegar is a dilute solution of acetic acid in water. It is uniform throughout and has no visible distinct phases.

Question 11 — Physical vs Chemical Changes

A physical change alters the form or appearance but not the chemical identity. A chemical change produces one or more new substances with different properties.

Question 11

Classify each of the following changes as physical or chemical.

  1. Food digesting in a stomach
  2. Explosion of gas in a mine
  3. Baking bread
  4. Casting a silver ring
  5. Scratching a wooden floor
Walkthrough

Key question to ask: Is a new substance formed, or does only the shape/state change?

(a) Food digesting — Chemical

Enzymes break complex molecules (proteins, fats, carbs) into simpler ones. New chemical substances are formed.

(b) Explosion of gas — Chemical

Combustion (rapid oxidation) converts fuel and oxygen into CO2, H2O, and energy. Entirely new substances are produced.

(c) Baking bread — Chemical

Yeast fermentation produces CO2, proteins denature, and Maillard reactions create new flavor/color compounds. New substances are formed.

(d) Casting a silver ring — Physical

Molten silver is poured into a mold. Only the shape changes — it is still silver (Ag). No new substance is formed.

(e) Scratching a wooden floor — Physical

The wood's surface is abraded, but the chemical composition of the wood is unchanged. Only the shape/texture is altered.

Question 12 — Volume Unit Conversion (µm³ to m³)

When converting cubic units, the conversion factor must be cubed because volume has three dimensions.

Question 12

The cell of an amoeba had an estimated volume of 1215 µm3 (cubic micrometers). Convert this volume to m3.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify the linear conversion factor.

1 µm = 1 × 10-6 m

Step 2 — Cube the conversion factor to handle cubic units.

\[ 1\ \mu\text{m} = 10^{-6}\ \text{m} \implies 1\ \mu\text{m}^3 = \left(10^{-6}\right)^3 \text{m}^3 = 10^{-18}\ \text{m}^3 \]

Step 3 — Apply the conversion.

\[ 1215\ \mu\text{m}^3 \times \frac{10^{-18}\ \text{m}^3}{1\ \mu\text{m}^3} = 1215 \times 10^{-18}\ \text{m}^3 \]

Step 4 — Express in proper scientific notation (4 sig figs).

\(1215 \times 10^{-18} = 1.215 \times 10^{-15}\)

Answer: 1.215 × 10-15 m3

Question 15 — Scientific Notation Conversions

Scientific notation writes a number as \(a \times 10^n\) where \(1 \le |a| < 10\). Move the decimal point and adjust the exponent accordingly.

Question 15

Convert each of the following to proper scientific notation.

  1. 33500 m
  2. 243.00 × 103 m
  3. 0.0000527 cm
  4. 0.0025 × 10-4 cm
Walkthrough

(a) 33500 m

Move decimal 4 places left: \(3.35 \times 10^4\) m.

Significant figures: 33500 has 3 sig figs (trailing zeros without decimal point are ambiguous; assume 3 here).

3.35 × 104 m

(b) 243.00 × 103 m

First express 243.00 in proper form: move decimal 2 places left → \(2.4300 \times 10^2\).

Then combine exponents: \(2.4300 \times 10^2 \times 10^3 = 2.4300 \times 10^5\) m.

The trailing zeros in 243.00 are significant (5 sig figs total).

2.4300 × 105 m

(c) 0.0000527 cm

Move decimal 5 places right: \(5.27 \times 10^{-5}\) cm. (3 sig figs)

5.27 × 10-5 cm

(d) 0.0025 × 10-4 cm

Express 0.0025 in proper form: move decimal 3 places right → \(2.5 \times 10^{-3}\).

Combine exponents: \(2.5 \times 10^{-3} \times 10^{-4} = 2.5 \times 10^{-7}\) cm. (2 sig figs)

2.5 × 10-7 cm

Question 16 — Mass Unit Conversion (g to mg)

The prefix milli- means 10-3, so 1 g = 1000 mg. Divide grams by 10-3 (or multiply by 1000) to convert.

Question 16

An aspirin tablet was analyzed and found to contain 0.275 g of acetylsalicylic acid. How many milligrams of acetylsalicylic acid does this equal?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Write the conversion factor.

1 g = 1000 mg, so \(\frac{1000\ \text{mg}}{1\ \text{g}}\).

Step 2 — Apply dimensional analysis.

\[ 0.275\ \text{g} \times \frac{1000\ \text{mg}}{1\ \text{g}} = 275\ \text{mg} \]

Step 3 — Check significant figures.

0.275 g has 3 sig figs. The conversion factor is exact. Answer: 3 sig figs.

Answer: 275 mg

Use SI prefix relationships: 1 kg = 103 g and 1 pg = 10-12 g.

Question 17

One atom of cadmium has a mass of approximately 1.867 × 10-22 g. Express this mass in (a) kilograms and (b) picograms.

Walkthrough

Question 18 — Accuracy and Precision

Accurate = close to the true/reference value (within 2%). Precise = measurements close to each other (all within 5% of each other).

Question 18

Classify each set of measurements as accurate, precise, both, or neither. (Accurate = within 2% of reference; precise = all measurements within 5% of each other.)

  1. Cookie weights: 17.58 g, 13.65 g, 19.82 g, 16.81 g. Reference: 15 g.
  2. Pipette volumes: 26.95 mL, 27.07 mL, 26.97 mL, 26.94 mL. Reference: 25 mL.
  3. Gold purity: 75.0012%, 75.0003%, 74.9998%, 75.0006%. Reference: 75.0%.
Walkthrough

(a) Cookie weights — ref = 15 g

Average = (17.58 + 13.65 + 19.82 + 16.81) / 4 = 67.86 / 4 = 16.97 g

% error = |16.97 - 15| / 15 × 100 = 13.1% → NOT accurate (>2%)

Range = 19.82 - 13.65 = 6.17 g; % spread = 6.17 / 16.97 × 100 = 36.4% → NOT precise (>5%)

(a) Neither accurate nor precise

(b) Pipette volumes — ref = 25 mL

Average = (26.95 + 27.07 + 26.97 + 26.94) / 4 = 107.93 / 4 = 26.98 mL

% error = |26.98 - 25| / 25 × 100 = 7.93% → NOT accurate (>2%)

Range = 27.07 - 26.94 = 0.13 mL; % spread = 0.13 / 26.98 × 100 = 0.48% → PRECISE (<5%)

(b) Precise, but not accurate

(c) Gold purity — ref = 75.0%

Average = (75.0012 + 75.0003 + 74.9998 + 75.0006) / 4 = 75.0005%

% error = |75.0005 - 75.0| / 75.0 × 100 = 0.00067% → ACCURATE (<2%)

Range = 75.0012 - 74.9998 = 0.0014%; % spread = 0.0014 / 75.0005 × 100 = 0.0019% → PRECISE (<5%)

(c) Both accurate and precise

Question 19 — Exact vs Inexact Numbers

Exact numbers are counted or defined (no uncertainty). Inexact numbers are measured and always have some uncertainty.

Question 19

State whether each number is exact or inexact.

  1. The interstate lane is 12 feet wide.
  2. The scuba diver's cylinder has a pressure of 3000 psi.
  3. There are 13 states that border Canada.
  4. The movie theater has a seating capacity of 200 persons.
Walkthrough

Key rule: If the number comes from a measurement (even if it looks round), it is inexact. If it comes from counting distinct objects or is a defined quantity, it is exact.

(a) 12 feet wide — Inexact

Lane width is a measured quantity. The actual width could be 11.8 or 12.2 ft. A measuring instrument was used, so uncertainty exists.

(b) 3000 psi — Inexact

Pressure is read from a gauge. Even though it appears to be a round number, it is a measured value with inherent uncertainty.

(c) 13 states — Exact

States bordering Canada is a counted quantity. You can enumerate all 13 with certainty (ME, NH, VT, NY, PA, OH, MI, MN, ND, MT, ID, WA, AK). No uncertainty.

(d) 200 seating capacity — Exact

The number of seats in a theater is a defined, counted quantity. The theater has exactly 200 seats by design.

StatementType
(a) 12 feet lane widthInexact
(b) 3000 psi pressureInexact
(c) 13 statesExact
(d) 200 seatsExact

Question 20 — Counting Significant Figures

Rules: non-zero digits are always significant; zeros between sig figs are significant; leading zeros are never significant; trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant.

Question 20

How many significant figures are in 0.000640 g?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify each digit and apply the rules.

0.000640

Leading zeros (0.000) — NOT significant (they only set the decimal place).

6 — significant (non-zero digit).

4 — significant (non-zero digit).

Trailing 0 after decimal point — significant (indicates the measurement was made to that precision).

Step 2 — Count the significant figures.

Significant digits: 6, 4, 0 → 3 significant figures.

Answer: 3 significant figures

Question 21 — Significant Figures in Subtraction and Division

For addition/subtraction: result is limited to the fewest decimal places. For multiplication/division: result has the fewest significant figures.

Question 21

The combined mass of a container plus acetone was 369.4 g. The empty container had a mass of 213.54 g. The volume of acetone was 197 cm3.

  1. Find the mass of acetone (369.4 - 213.54 g) with the correct sig figs.
  2. Calculate the density of acetone (mass / 197 cm3) with the correct sig figs.
Walkthrough

(a) Subtraction: 369.4 - 213.54

369.4 — limited to the tenths place (1 decimal place).

213.54 — limited to the hundredths place (2 decimal places).

Result must be rounded to the tenths place (fewer decimal places).

\[ 369.4 - 213.54 = 155.86 \xrightarrow{\text{round to tenths}} 155.9\ \text{g} \]

Mass of acetone = 155.9 g

(b) Division: 155.9 / 197

155.9 has 4 sig figs. 197 has 3 sig figs.

Result must have 3 sig figs (fewest).

\[ \frac{155.9\ \text{g}}{197\ \text{cm}^3} = 0.79136... \xrightarrow{\text{3 sig figs}} 0.791\ \text{g/cm}^3 \]

Density = 0.791 g/cm3

Question 22 — Significant Figures in Mixed Operations

Apply addition/subtraction rules (decimal places) and multiplication/division rules (sig fig count) independently, tracking the limiting value at each step.

Question 22

Carry out the following operations and express each answer in scientific notation with the correct number of significant figures.

  1. 2.26 + 4.62 + 5.9 + 8.198
  2. \(\dfrac{0.0250 \times 0.29}{16.20 \times 0.1040}\)
Walkthrough

(a) Addition: 2.26 + 4.62 + 5.9 + 8.198

Find the fewest decimal places among all values:

2.26 → 2 decimals | 4.62 → 2 decimals | 5.9 → 1 decimal | 8.198 → 3 decimals

Limiting value: 5.9 (1 decimal place). Round result to tenths.

\[ 2.26 + 4.62 + 5.9 + 8.198 = 20.978 \xrightarrow{\text{round to tenths}} 21.0 \]

Convert to scientific notation: \(2.10 \times 10^1\) (3 sig figs)

2.10 × 101

(b) Division of products: (0.0250 × 0.29) / (16.20 × 0.1040)

Numerator: 0.0250 (3 sig figs) × 0.29 (2 sig figs) = 0.00725 → limited to 2 sig figs = 0.0073

Denominator: 16.20 (4 sig figs) × 0.1040 (4 sig figs) = 1.6848 → 4 sig figs = 1.685

Division: 0.00725 / 1.6848 = 0.004303 → limited by numerator's 2 sig figs

\[ \frac{0.0250 \times 0.29}{16.20 \times 0.1040} = \frac{0.00725}{1.6848} = 0.004303... \xrightarrow{\text{2 sig figs}} 0.0043 = 4.3 \times 10^{-3} \]

4.3 × 10-3

Question 23 — Unit Conversion (lb to kg)

Use dimensional analysis with the exact conversion: 1 lb = 453.59237 g. Since the conversion is exact, significant figures are determined only by the measured value.

Question 23

A bottle of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) was found to contain 1.21 lb. Express the amount in kilograms. (1 lb = 453.59237 g exactly.)

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Convert lb to grams.

\[ 1.21\ \text{lb} \times \frac{453.59237\ \text{g}}{1\ \text{lb}} = 548.847\ \text{g} \]

Step 2 — Convert grams to kilograms.

\[ 548.847\ \text{g} \times \frac{1\ \text{kg}}{1000\ \text{g}} = 0.548847\ \text{kg} \]

Step 3 — Apply significant figures.

1.21 lb has 3 sig figs. The conversion factors are exact. Round to 3 sig figs.

0.548847 → 0.549 kg

Answer: 0.549 kg

Question 24 — Unit Conversion (feet to km)

Chain multiple exact conversion factors: feet → inches → cm → m → km. Use 1 in = 2.54 cm exactly.

Question 24

An altitude of 33,475 feet is equivalent to how many kilometers? (1 in = 2.54 cm exactly.)

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Set up the full conversion chain.

\[ 33{,}475\ \text{ft} \times \frac{12\ \text{in}}{1\ \text{ft}} \times \frac{2.54\ \text{cm}}{1\ \text{in}} \times \frac{1\ \text{m}}{100\ \text{cm}} \times \frac{1\ \text{km}}{1000\ \text{m}} \]

Step 2 — Combine numerator values.

\(33{,}475 \times 12 \times 2.54 = 33{,}475 \times 30.48 = 1{,}020{,}318\ \text{cm}\)

Step 3 — Divide by 105 to reach km.

Question 25 — Dalton's Atomic Theory

Dalton's atomic theory (early 1800s) provided the first scientific framework for understanding matter in terms of atoms.

Question 25

Summarize the four postulates of Dalton's atomic theory.

Walkthrough

Postulate I — All matter is made of atoms, which are tiny and indivisible.

Atoms are the smallest particles of matter. They cannot be broken down by chemical means. (We now know atoms have substructure, but for chemistry purposes this holds.)

Postulate II — All atoms of a given element are identical; atoms of different elements are different.

Every carbon atom has the same properties; a carbon atom differs from an oxygen atom. (Isotopes are a modern refinement to this postulate.)

Postulate III — Chemical reactions cannot create or destroy atoms, and cannot change atoms of one element into atoms of another.

This is the atomic basis of the law of conservation of mass. Atoms are simply rearranged in reactions.

Postulate IV — A compound is a combination of two or more different types of atoms in a fixed, small whole-number ratio.

Water is always H2O (2:1 ratio). This explains the law of definite proportions and the law of multiple proportions.

Question 36 — Similarity of Chemical Properties

Elements in the same group (column) of the periodic table have similar chemical and physical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons.

Question 36

Which set of elements would you expect to exhibit greater similarity in chemical and physical properties: Be, B, and O — or — Sr, Mg, and Ca?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify the periodic table groups of each element.

Be = Group 2, B = Group 13, O = Group 16 → all in different groups, different periods.

Sr = Group 2, Mg = Group 2, Ca = Group 2 → all alkaline earth metals, same group.

Step 2 — Apply the periodic law.

Elements in the same group share valence electron configuration (all have 2 valence electrons: ns2) and therefore exhibit similar reactivity, ionization energies, and bonding behavior.

Step 3 — Conclude.

Be, B, and O span different groups — their properties differ significantly. Sr, Mg, and Ca are all Group 2 alkaline earth metals and will behave very similarly.

Sr, Mg, and Ca exhibit greater similarity.

Question 53 — Dilution and Concentration by Evaporation

When solutions are diluted or concentrated: moles of solute stay constant. Use \(M_1 V_1 = M_2 V_2\).

Question 53

Determine the concentration of a 1.00 M NaCl solution after each change.

  1. Exactly 1.00 L of the solution is mixed with 0.45 L of water.
  2. Exactly 1.00 L of the solution evaporates down to 0.29 L.
Walkthrough

(a) Dilution: add 0.45 L water to 1.00 L of 1.00 M NaCl

Moles of NaCl = 1.00 M × 1.00 L = 1.00 mol (constant)

New volume = 1.00 + 0.45 = 1.45 L

\[ M_2 = \frac{n}{V_2} = \frac{1.00\ \text{mol}}{1.45\ \text{L}} = 0.6897\ \text{M} \]

0.690 M

(b) Evaporation: 1.00 L reduces to 0.29 L

Moles of NaCl = 1.00 mol (water evaporates, solute stays)

New volume = 0.29 L

\[ M_2 = \frac{1.00\ \text{mol}}{0.29\ \text{L}} = 3.45\ \text{M} \]

Round to 2 sig figs (limited by 0.29 L).

3.4 M

Question 54 — Ion Concentrations from Molarity of Ionic Compound

Each ion's concentration = compound molarity × number of that ion per formula unit.

Question 54

Determine the concentrations of each ion present in 0.045 M Mg(NO3)2.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Write the dissociation equation.

Mg(NO3)2 → Mg2+ + 2 NO3−

Step 2 — Find each ion's concentration.

One Mg2+ per formula unit:

\[ [\text{Mg}^{2+}] = 1 \times 0.045\ \text{M} = 0.045\ \text{M} \]

Two NO3− per formula unit:

\[ [\text{NO}_3^-] = 2 \times 0.045\ \text{M} = 0.090\ \text{M} \]
