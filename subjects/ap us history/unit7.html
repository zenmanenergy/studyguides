AP US History — Unit 7: Period 7 (1890–1945)

Progressive Era (1890s–1920)

  • Muckrakers: investigative journalists exposed social problems — Upton Sinclair (The Jungle, meatpacking), Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil), Jacob Riis (How the Other Half Lives, tenements)
  • TR (Theodore Roosevelt): trust-busting (Northern Securities Case), Square Deal, conservation, Pure Food and Drug Act (1906)
  • Taft: more trusts busted; alienated progressives; split Republican Party
  • Wilson: New Freedom; Federal Reserve (1913), Clayton Antitrust Act, income tax (16th Amendment)

World War I and Its Aftermath

  • US neutral 1914–1917; German unrestricted submarine warfare + Zimmermann Telegram pushed US in
  • Wilson's 14 Points: self-determination, open diplomacy, freedom of seas, League of Nations
  • Treaty of Versailles (1919): Senate rejected it; US never joined League of Nations (isolationism)
  • Great Migration: 1.6 million Black Americans moved North for factory jobs and to escape Jim Crow (1910–1940)
  • Red Scare (1919–20): fear of communism after Russian Revolution; Palmer Raids; Sacco and Vanzetti

The 1920s

  • Consumer culture: automobiles, radio, appliances; installment buying; advertising boom
  • Harlem Renaissance: flowering of Black literature, art, music — Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Duke Ellington
  • Prohibition: 18th Amendment; rise of organized crime (Al Capone); repealed by 21st (1933)
  • Nativism: Immigration Act of 1924 established national-origins quotas; severely restricted Southern/Eastern European and Asian immigration
  • KKK revival; fundamentalism vs. modernism (Scopes Trial, 1925)
  • Stock speculation and overextension set the stage for the 1929 crash

Great Depression and New Deal

  • Causes of Depression: stock market crash (Oct 1929), bank failures, Smoot-Hawley Tariff, falling farm prices, overproduction, income inequality
  • Hoover: relied on voluntary cooperation and Reconstruction Finance Corporation; seen as too passive
  • FDR's New Deal: relief, recovery, reform — FDIC, Social Security, SEC, CCC, TVA, WPA
  • Critics: Huey Long (Share Our Wealth), Father Coughlin (too conservative); Supreme Court struck down early New Deal programs
  • Significance: expanded role of federal government; set expectations for government intervention in economy
New Deal coalition: labor unions, urban immigrants, Black voters (in North), White Southerners, farmers — sustained Democratic dominance until the 1960s

World War II

  • US neutrality (1939–41): Lend-Lease aided Britain; isolationism vs. interventionism debate
  • Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941): Japanese attack; US declared war on Japan; Germany declared war on US
  • Japanese American internment: Executive Order 9066; 120,000 Japanese Americans relocated; Korematsu v. US (1944) upheld it
  • Homefront: wartime production; women in workforce ("Rosie the Riveter"); rationing; war bonds
  • Double V campaign: Black Americans fought for democracy abroad and at home
  • D-Day (June 6, 1944); Germany surrendered May 1945; atomic bombs on Hiroshima/Nagasaki August 1945 → Japan surrendered
