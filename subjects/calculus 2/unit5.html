Topic 1 — Complete the Table Using Parametric Equations

Evaluate \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) at each given value of \(t\), record the results, and identify the underlying Cartesian curve by eliminating the parameter.

Question 1

Given \(x = t + 1\) and \(y = t^2 - 4\), complete the table for \(t = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2\). Then eliminate the parameter to identify the curve.

\(t\)\(x = t+1\)\(y = t^2-4\)Point \((x,y)\)
????
Walkthrough

Step 1 — Substitute each \(t\):

\(t\)\(x = t+1\)\(y = t^2-4\)Point \((x,y)\)
\(-2\)\(-1\)\(0\)\((-1,\;0)\)
\(-1\)\(0\)\(-3\)\((0,\;-3)\)
\(0\)\(1\)\(-4\)\((1,\;-4)\)
\(1\)\(2\)\(-3\)\((2,\;-3)\)
\(2\)\(3\)\(0\)\((3,\;0)\)

Step 2 — Eliminate the parameter:

From \(x = t+1\), solve for \(t\): \(t = x - 1\).

Substitute into \(y\): \(y = (x-1)^2 - 4\).

\[ y = (x-1)^2 - 4 \]

This is an upward-opening parabola with vertex at \((1, -4)\). The table confirms it: the points are symmetric about \(x = 1\).

Question 2

Given \(x = 3\cos t\) and \(y = 3\sin t\), complete the table for \(t = 0,\; \frac{\pi}{6},\; \frac{\pi}{4},\; \frac{\pi}{3},\; \frac{\pi}{2}\). Then identify the curve.

\(t\)\(x = 3\cos t\)\(y = 3\sin t\)Point (approx)
????
Walkthrough

Step 1 — Evaluate using exact values and decimal approximations:

\(t\)\(x = 3\cos t\)\(y = 3\sin t\)Point (approx)
\(0\)\(3\)\(0\)\((3.00,\;0.00)\)
\(\pi/6\)\(\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\)\(\frac{3}{2}\)\((2.60,\;1.50)\)
\(\pi/4\)\(\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\)\(\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\)\((2.12,\;2.12)\)
\(\pi/3\)\(\frac{3}{2}\)\(\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\)\((1.50,\;2.60)\)
\(\pi/2\)\(0\)\(3\)\((0.00,\;3.00)\)

Step 2 — Eliminate the parameter:

Solve: \(\cos t = \dfrac{x}{3}\) and \(\sin t = \dfrac{y}{3}\).

Apply the Pythagorean identity \(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1\):

\[ \frac{x^2}{9} + \frac{y^2}{9} = 1 \implies x^2 + y^2 = 9 \]

This is a circle of radius 3 centered at the origin. The table traces the first-quadrant arc from \((3,0)\) to \((0,3)\).

Question 3

Given \(x = 2t - 1\) and \(y = t^2 + t\), complete the table for \(t = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2\). Then eliminate the parameter.

\(t\)\(x = 2t-1\)\(y = t^2+t\)Point \((x,y)\)

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Substitute each \(t\):

\(t\)\(x = 2t-1\)\(y = t^2+t\)Point \((x,y)\)
\(-2\)\(-5\)\(2\)\((-5,\;2)\)
\(-1\)\(-3\)\(0\)\((-3,\;0)\)
\(0\)\(-1\)\(0\)\((-1,\;0)\)
\(1\)\(1\)\(2\)\((1,\;2)\)
\(2\)\(3\)\(6\)\((3,\;6)\)

Step 2 — Eliminate the parameter:

From \(x = 2t - 1\), solve: \(t = \dfrac{x+1}{2}\).

Substitute into \(y = t^2 + t = t(t+1)\):

\[ y = \frac{x+1}{2}\cdot\frac{x+3}{2} = \frac{(x+1)(x+3)}{4} \]

This is a parabola. Notice that \(y = 0\) at \(x = -1\) and \(x = -3\) (confirmed in the table), giving intercepts and a vertex at \(x = -2\), \(y = -\frac{1}{4}\).

Topic 2 — Construct Parametric Equations for a Line

A line through point \((x_0, y_0)\) with direction vector \((a, b)\) has parametric equations \(x = x_0 + at,\; y = y_0 + bt\). Note: the representation is not unique — any scalar multiple of the direction vector works.

Question 1

Construct parametric equations for the line passing through \((2, 5)\) with slope \(-3\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify a direction vector from the slope.

Slope \(-3\) means: for every 1 unit right, go 3 units down. Direction vector: \((1, -3)\).

Step 2 — Write parametric equations using the point and direction.

\[ x = 2 + t, \qquad y = 5 - 3t \]

Step 3 — Verify.

\(\dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \dfrac{-3}{1} = -3\) — slope is correct. At \(t=0\): point is \((2, 5)\) — correct.

Answer: \(x = 2 + t,\quad y = 5 - 3t\)

Question 2

Construct parametric equations for the line passing through \((-1, 4)\) and \((3, -2)\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find the direction vector.

Direction: \((3 - (-1),\; -2 - 4) = (4, -6)\). We can simplify by dividing by 2: direction \((2, -3)\).

Step 2 — Write equations using either point (use \((-1, 4)\)).

\[ x = -1 + 2t, \qquad y = 4 - 3t \]

Step 3 — Verify both points are on the curve.

At \(t = 0\): \((-1, 4)\) — correct.

At \(t = 2\): \(x = -1+4=3\), \(y = 4-6=-2\) — correct.

Answer: \(x = -1 + 2t,\quad y = 4 - 3t\)

Question 3

Construct parametric equations for the line with \(x\)-intercept \((5, 0)\) and \(y\)-intercept \((0, -3)\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify slope from intercepts.

Slope \(= \dfrac{-3 - 0}{0 - 5} = \dfrac{-3}{-5} = \dfrac{3}{5}\). Direction vector: \((5, 3)\) (or any scalar multiple).

Step 2 — Use the \(x\)-intercept \((5, 0)\) as base point.

\[ x = 5 + 5t, \qquad y = 3t \]

Alternate form using the \(y\)-intercept \((0, -3)\) as base point:

\[ x = 5t, \qquad y = -3 + 3t \]

Step 3 — Verify.

For the second form: \(t = 0 \Rightarrow (0, -3)\) — correct. \(t = 1 \Rightarrow (5, 0)\) — correct.

Answer (one valid form): \(x = 5t,\quad y = -3 + 3t\)

Topic 3 — Parametric Modeling

Parametric equations naturally describe motion in the plane where both position coordinates depend on time \(t\). Key physics facts: horizontal velocity is constant, vertical position under gravity is \(y = y_0 + v_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) with \(g = 32\) ft/s\(^2\).

Question 1

A projectile is launched from the ground at a 45-degree angle with an initial speed of 64 ft/s. Write parametric equations for its position. Find the time of flight, maximum height, and range.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Decompose initial velocity.

\(v_x = 64\cos 45^\circ = 64 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = 32\sqrt{2}\) ft/s

\(v_y = 64\sin 45^\circ = 32\sqrt{2}\) ft/s

Step 2 — Write parametric equations.

\[ x(t) = 32\sqrt{2}\, t, \qquad y(t) = 32\sqrt{2}\, t - 16t^2 \]

Step 3 — Time of flight. Set \(y = 0\):

\(t(32\sqrt{2} - 16t) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 0\) or \(t = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83\) sec

Step 4 — Maximum height. Occurs at \(t = \sqrt{2}\) (midpoint of flight):

\(y(\sqrt{2}) = 32\sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{2} - 16\cdot 2 = 64 - 32 = 32\) ft

Step 5 — Range.

\(x(2\sqrt{2}) = 32\sqrt{2}\cdot 2\sqrt{2} = 32 \cdot 4 = 128\) ft

Equations: \(x = 32\sqrt{2}\,t,\; y = 32\sqrt{2}\,t - 16t^2\). Time of flight: \(2\sqrt{2}\) s. Max height: 32 ft. Range: 128 ft.

Question 2

A ball rolls horizontally off a table 4 ft high at 6 ft/s. Write parametric equations for its position. How long until it hits the ground? How far from the table does it land?

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Set up equations. Initial position: \((0, 4)\). No vertical launch component.

\[ x(t) = 6t, \qquad y(t) = 4 - 16t^2 \]

Step 2 — Time to hit ground. Set \(y = 0\):

\(4 - 16t^2 = 0 \Rightarrow t^2 = \dfrac{1}{4} \Rightarrow t = \dfrac{1}{2}\) sec

Step 3 — Horizontal distance.

\(x\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = 6\cdot\dfrac{1}{2} = 3\) ft

The ball hits the ground after 0.5 sec and lands 3 ft from the base of the table.

Question 3

A Ferris wheel has radius 20 ft and its center is 25 ft above the ground. It completes one full revolution every 60 seconds. A rider starts at the lowest point. Write parametric equations for the rider's position \((x(t), y(t))\) where \(t\) is in seconds.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify the angular speed.

One revolution = \(2\pi\) radians in 60 sec, so \(\omega = \dfrac{2\pi}{60} = \dfrac{\pi}{30}\) rad/s.

Step 2 — Determine starting angle.

The standard parameterization puts \(\theta = 0\) at the right side. The bottom of the wheel is at angle \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\) (or equivalently, \(\theta = \omega t - \frac{\pi}{2}\)).

Center of wheel: \((0, 25)\). At \(t = 0\) the rider is at \((0, 5)\) (directly below center).

Step 3 — Write parametric equations.

\[ x(t) = 20\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi t}{30}\right), \qquad y(t) = 25 - 20\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi t}{30}\right) \]

Step 4 — Verify key positions.

\(t = 0\): \(x = 0,\; y = 25 - 20 = 5\) ft — bottom of wheel. Correct.

\(t = 30\): \(x = 0,\; y = 25 + 20 = 45\) ft — top of wheel. Correct.

\(t = 15\): \(x = 20,\; y = 25\) ft — rightmost point. Correct.

Answer: \(x(t) = 20\sin\!\bigl(\frac{\pi t}{30}\bigr),\quad y(t) = 25 - 20\cos\!\bigl(\frac{\pi t}{30}\bigr)\)

Topic 4 — Find Horizontal Tangent Lines

A parametric curve has a horizontal tangent where \(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0\) and \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} \ne 0\). If both equal zero at the same \(t\), that point requires further analysis.

Question 1

Find all horizontal tangent lines to the curve \(x = t^2 - 2t,\quad y = t^3 - 3t^2\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Differentiate both components.

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = 2t - 2, \qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = 3t^2 - 6t = 3t(t-2) \]

Step 2 — Set \(\frac{dy}{dt} = 0\).

\(3t(t-2) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 0\) or \(t = 2\).

Step 3 — Confirm \(\frac{dx}{dt} \ne 0\) at each.

At \(t = 0\): \(\frac{dx}{dt} = -2 \ne 0\). Horizontal tangent exists.

At \(t = 2\): \(\frac{dx}{dt} = 2 \ne 0\). Horizontal tangent exists.

Step 4 — Find the Cartesian points.

\(t=0\): \(x = 0,\; y = 0\) → tangent line \(y = 0\).

\(t=2\): \(x = 4-4=0,\; y = 8-12=-4\) → tangent line \(y = -4\).

Horizontal tangent lines: \(y = 0\) and \(y = -4\).

Question 2

Find all horizontal tangent lines to the curve \(x = \cos t,\quad y = \sin(2t)\) for \(t \in [0, 2\pi)\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Differentiate.

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = -\sin t, \qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2\cos(2t) \]

Step 2 — Set \(\frac{dy}{dt} = 0\).

\(2\cos(2t) = 0 \Rightarrow 2t = \frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2}, \frac{5\pi}{2}, \frac{7\pi}{2} \Rightarrow t = \frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{3\pi}{4}, \frac{5\pi}{4}, \frac{7\pi}{4}\).

Step 3 — Check \(\frac{dx}{dt} = -\sin t \ne 0\) at each.

\(\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \ne 0\) — confirmed for all four values (none are multiples of \(\pi\)).

Step 4 — Find the \(y\)-coordinates.

\(t = \frac{\pi}{4}\): \(y = \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 1\).

\(t = \frac{3\pi}{4}\): \(y = \sin\!\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) = -1\).

\(t = \frac{5\pi}{4}\): \(y = \sin\!\left(\frac{5\pi}{2}\right) = 1\) (same line as \(t = \frac{\pi}{4}\)).

\(t = \frac{7\pi}{4}\): \(y = \sin\!\left(\frac{7\pi}{2}\right) = -1\) (same line as \(t = \frac{3\pi}{4}\)).

Horizontal tangent lines: \(y = 1\) and \(y = -1\).

Question 3

Find all horizontal tangent lines to the curve \(x = t^3 - 3t + 1,\quad y = t^2 - 1\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Differentiate.

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = 3t^2 - 3, \qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t \]

Step 2 — Set \(\frac{dy}{dt} = 0\).

\(2t = 0 \Rightarrow t = 0\).

Step 3 — Check \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) at \(t = 0\).

\(\frac{dx}{dt}\big|_{t=0} = 3(0)^2 - 3 = -3 \ne 0\). Horizontal tangent confirmed.

Step 4 — Find the point.

\(x = 0 - 0 + 1 = 1,\quad y = 0 - 1 = -1\). Point: \((1, -1)\).

Horizontal tangent line: \(y = -1\).

Topic 5 — Find dy/dx for a Parametric Curve

For a parametric curve, the slope of the tangent at a given \(t\) is:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} \qquad \left(\frac{dx}{dt} \ne 0\right) \]
Question 1

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for the curve \(x = t^3 - t,\quad y = t^2 + 2t\) at \(t = 1\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Differentiate each component.

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = 3t^2 - 1, \qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t + 2 \]

Step 2 — Evaluate at \(t = 1\).

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\big|_{t=1} = 3(1)^2 - 1 = 2\).

\(\dfrac{dy}{dt}\big|_{t=1} = 2(1) + 2 = 4\).

Step 3 — Compute \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \]

The slope of the tangent line at \(t = 1\) is \(2\).

Question 2

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for the curve \(x = e^t,\quad y = t\,e^t\) at \(t = 0\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Differentiate.

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = e^t\).

\(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = e^t + t\,e^t = e^t(1 + t)\)   (product rule).

Step 2 — Evaluate at \(t = 0\).

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\big|_0 = e^0 = 1\).

\(\dfrac{dy}{dt}\big|_0 = e^0(1 + 0) = 1\).

Step 3 — Compute.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{1} = 1 \]

The slope of the tangent at \(t = 0\) (the point \((1, 0)\)) is \(1\).

Question 3

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for the curve \(x = \sin(2t),\quad y = \cos t\) at \(t = \dfrac{\pi}{6}\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Differentiate.

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = 2\cos(2t)\).

\(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = -\sin t\).

Step 2 — Evaluate at \(t = \pi/6\).

\(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\big|_{\pi/6} = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = 2\cdot\frac{1}{2} = 1\).

\(\dfrac{dy}{dt}\big|_{\pi/6} = -\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = -\frac{1}{2}\).

Step 3 — Compute.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-1/2}{1} = -\frac{1}{2} \]

The slope of the tangent at \(t = \pi/6\) is \(-\dfrac{1}{2}\).

Topic 6 — Convert a Polar Point to Cartesian

Given a polar point \((r, \theta)\), convert using:

\[ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta \]

A negative \(r\) means the point lies in the direction opposite to \(\theta\).

Question 1

Convert the polar point \(\left(4,\; \dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)\) to Cartesian coordinates.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Apply conversion formulas.

\(x = 4\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) = 4\cdot\dfrac{1}{2} = 2\).

\(y = 4\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) = 4\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 2\sqrt{3}\).

Cartesian point: \(\left(2,\; 2\sqrt{3}\right) \approx (2,\; 3.46)\).

Question 2

Convert the polar point \(\left(6,\; \dfrac{5\pi}{6}\right)\) to Cartesian coordinates.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Recall exact values at \(5\pi/6\).

\(\cos\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\right) = -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\),   \(\sin\!\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\right) = \dfrac{1}{2}\).

Step 2 — Apply formulas.

\(x = 6\cdot\left(-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) = -3\sqrt{3}\).

\(y = 6\cdot\dfrac{1}{2} = 3\).

Cartesian point: \(\left(-3\sqrt{3},\; 3\right) \approx (-5.20,\; 3)\).

Question 3

Convert the polar point \(\left(-3,\; \dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)\) to Cartesian coordinates.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Note that \(r = -3\) (negative). The point is reflected through the origin relative to \(\theta = \pi/4\).

Step 2 — Apply formulas (the sign on \(r\) carries through automatically).

\(x = -3\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right) = -3\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = -\dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

\(y = -3\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right) = -3\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = -\dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

Step 3 — Verify direction. The positive version \((3, \pi/4)\) would give \(\left(\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\). The negative \(r\) gives the reflected point in the third quadrant. Confirmed.

Cartesian point: \(\left(-\dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2},\; -\dfrac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) \approx (-2.12,\; -2.12)\).

Topic 7 — Convert a Polar Equation to Rectangular

Use the substitutions \(r^2 = x^2 + y^2\), \(r\cos\theta = x\), \(r\sin\theta = y\). Multiply through by \(r\) when needed to create \(r^2\) or \(r\cos\theta\) terms.

Question 1

Convert \(r = 4\sin\theta\) to rectangular form and identify the curve.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Multiply both sides by \(r\).

\[ r^2 = 4r\sin\theta \]

Step 2 — Substitute \(r^2 = x^2+y^2\) and \(r\sin\theta = y\).

\[ x^2 + y^2 = 4y \]

Step 3 — Complete the square in \(y\).

\[ x^2 + y^2 - 4y = 0 \implies x^2 + (y-2)^2 = 4 \]

This is a circle with center \((0, 2)\) and radius \(2\).

Question 2

Convert \(r = \dfrac{3}{1 - \cos\theta}\) to rectangular form and identify the curve.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Multiply both sides by \((1 - \cos\theta)\).

\[ r - r\cos\theta = 3 \]

Step 2 — Substitute \(r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) and \(r\cos\theta = x\).

\[ \sqrt{x^2+y^2} - x = 3 \implies \sqrt{x^2+y^2} = x + 3 \]

Step 3 — Square both sides (valid when \(x \ge -3\)).

\[ x^2 + y^2 = x^2 + 6x + 9 \implies y^2 = 6x + 9 \]

This is a parabola opening to the right: \(y^2 = 6\!\left(x + \frac{3}{2}\right)\), vertex at \(\left(-\frac{3}{2}, 0\right)\).

Question 3

Convert \(r = \dfrac{2}{3 - 2\cos\theta}\) to rectangular form and identify the curve.

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Multiply both sides by \((3 - 2\cos\theta)\).

\[ 3r - 2r\cos\theta = 2 \]

Step 2 — Substitute.

\[ 3\sqrt{x^2+y^2} - 2x = 2 \implies 3\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = 2x + 2 \]

Step 3 — Square both sides.

\[ 9(x^2+y^2) = (2x+2)^2 = 4x^2 + 8x + 4 \]

Step 4 — Simplify.

\[ 5x^2 + 9y^2 - 8x - 4 = 0 \]

Since both \(x^2\) and \(y^2\) terms are positive but have different coefficients, this is an ellipse. (It can be brought to standard form by completing the square.)

Topic 8 — Find the Slope of the Tangent to a Polar Curve

Treat \(r = f(\theta)\) as a parametric curve with \(\theta\) as the parameter: \(x = r\cos\theta,\; y = r\sin\theta\). Then:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{r'\sin\theta + r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta - r\sin\theta}, \qquad r' = \frac{dr}{d\theta} \]
Question 1

Find the slope of the tangent to the cardioid \(r = 2 + 2\cos\theta\) at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find \(r\) and \(r'\) at \(\theta = \pi/2\).

\(r = 2 + 2\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 2 + 0 = 2\).

\(r' = -2\sin\theta \Rightarrow r'\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -2\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -2\).

Step 2 — Compute numerator and denominator separately.

Numerator: \(r'\sin\theta + r\cos\theta = (-2)(1) + (2)(0) = -2\).

Denominator: \(r'\cos\theta - r\sin\theta = (-2)(0) - (2)(1) = -2\).

Step 3 — Compute slope.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-2}{-2} = 1 \]

The tangent line to the cardioid at \(\theta = \pi/2\) has slope \(1\).

Question 2

Find the slope of the tangent to \(r = 3\sin\theta\) at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{6}\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find \(r\) and \(r'\) at \(\theta = \pi/6\).

\(r = 3\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = 3\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{2}\).

\(r' = 3\cos\theta \Rightarrow r'\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = 3\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\).

Step 2 — Compute numerator and denominator.

Numerator: \(\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} + \frac{3}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{4} + \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{4} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\).

Denominator: \(\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \frac{3}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{9}{4} - \frac{3}{4} = \frac{6}{4} = \frac{3}{2}\).

Step 3 — Compute slope.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}/2}{3/2} = \sqrt{3} \]

The slope of the tangent at \(\theta = \pi/6\) is \(\sqrt{3}\) (corresponding to a 60-degree angle).

Question 3

Find the slope of the tangent to the rose \(r = \sin(2\theta)\) at \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{4}\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find \(r\) and \(r'\) at \(\theta = \pi/4\).

\(r = \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 1\).

\(r' = 2\cos(2\theta) \Rightarrow r'\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 0\).

Step 2 — Compute numerator and denominator.

Numerator: \(0\cdot\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) + 1\cdot\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

Denominator: \(0\cdot\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) - 1\cdot\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\).

Step 3 — Compute slope.

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\sqrt{2}/2}{-\sqrt{2}/2} = -1 \]

The slope at the tip of the petal at \(\theta = \pi/4\) is \(-1\).

Topic 9 — Find the Arc Length of a Polar Curve

The arc length of \(r = f(\theta)\) from \(\theta = \alpha\) to \(\theta = \beta\) is:

\[ L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}\, d\theta \]
Question 1

Find the arc length of \(r = 4\) from \(\theta = 0\) to \(\theta = \pi\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify \(r\) and \(r'\).

\(r = 4\) (constant), so \(r' = 0\).

Step 2 — Set up and evaluate the integral.

\[ L = \int_0^\pi \sqrt{4^2 + 0^2}\, d\theta = \int_0^\pi 4\, d\theta = 4\pi \]

Step 3 — Sanity check. \(r = 4\) is a circle of radius 4 with circumference \(8\pi\). The arc from \(0\) to \(\pi\) is a semicircle, so \(L = 4\pi\). Confirmed.

\(L = 4\pi\)

Question 2

Find the arc length of \(r = e^{2\theta}\) from \(\theta = 0\) to \(\theta = \pi\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find \(r'\).

\(r' = 2e^{2\theta}\).

Step 2 — Simplify the integrand.

\[ r^2 + (r')^2 = e^{4\theta} + 4e^{4\theta} = 5e^{4\theta} \implies \sqrt{5e^{4\theta}} = \sqrt{5}\, e^{2\theta} \]

Step 3 — Integrate.

\[ L = \int_0^\pi \sqrt{5}\, e^{2\theta}\, d\theta = \sqrt{5}\left[\frac{e^{2\theta}}{2}\right]_0^\pi = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\left(e^{2\pi} - 1\right) \]

\(L = \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\!\left(e^{2\pi} - 1\right) \approx 303.2\)

Question 3

Find the arc length of \(r = 2\cos\theta\) from \(\theta = 0\) to \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find \(r'\).

\(r' = -2\sin\theta\).

Step 2 — Simplify the integrand using a Pythagorean identity.

\[ r^2 + (r')^2 = 4\cos^2\theta + 4\sin^2\theta = 4(\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta) = 4 \]

Step 3 — Integrate.

\[ L = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{4}\, d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/2} 2\, d\theta = 2\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \pi \]

Step 4 — Sanity check. \(r = 2\cos\theta\) is a circle of diameter 2 (radius 1) centered at \((1, 0)\), with circumference \(2\pi\). The interval \(\theta = 0\) to \(\pi/2\) traces one full semicircle, giving \(L = \pi\). Confirmed.

\(L = \pi\)

Topic 10 — Construct a Polar Equation for a Conic Section

With a focus at the origin, all three conics fit the unified form:

\[ r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\cos\theta} \qquad \text{or} \qquad r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\sin\theta} \]

where \(e\) is eccentricity and \(d\) is the distance from focus to directrix. The sign and trig function depend on the orientation of the directrix:

Directrix locationForm
Right of focus: \(x = d\)\(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}\)
Left of focus: \(x = -d\)\(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 - e\cos\theta}\)
Above focus: \(y = d\)\(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 + e\sin\theta}\)
Below focus: \(y = -d\)\(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 - e\sin\theta}\)
Question 1

Construct a polar equation for the parabola with focus at the origin and directrix \(x = 4\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify \(e\) and \(d\).

A parabola has eccentricity \(e = 1\). The directrix \(x = 4\) is to the right of the focus, so \(d = 4\).

Step 2 — Select the correct form.

Directrix to the right: use \(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}\).

Step 3 — Substitute.

\[ r = \frac{(1)(4)}{1 + (1)\cos\theta} = \frac{4}{1 + \cos\theta} \]

\(r = \dfrac{4}{1 + \cos\theta}\)

Question 2

Construct a polar equation for the ellipse with eccentricity \(\dfrac{1}{2}\), focus at the origin, and directrix \(y = -6\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify \(e\) and \(d\).

\(e = \dfrac{1}{2}\), \(d = 6\). The directrix \(y = -6\) is below the focus.

Step 2 — Select the correct form.

Directrix below: use \(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 - e\sin\theta}\).

Step 3 — Substitute.

\[ r = \frac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot 6}{1 - \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta} = \frac{3}{1 - \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta} \]

Step 4 — Clear the fraction in the denominator (optional).

\[ r = \frac{6}{2 - \sin\theta} \]

Step 5 — Confirm it is an ellipse. Since \(e = \frac{1}{2} < 1\), yes.

\(r = \dfrac{6}{2 - \sin\theta}\)

Question 3

Construct a polar equation for the hyperbola with eccentricity \(2\), focus at the origin, and directrix \(x = -3\).

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify \(e\) and \(d\).

\(e = 2\), and the directrix \(x = -3\) is to the left of the focus, so \(d = 3\).

Step 2 — Select the correct form.

Directrix to the left: use \(r = \dfrac{ed}{1 - e\cos\theta}\).

Step 3 — Substitute.

\[ r = \frac{(2)(3)}{1 - (2)\cos\theta} = \frac{6}{1 - 2\cos\theta} \]

Step 4 — Confirm it is a hyperbola. Since \(e = 2 > 1\), yes. The curve has two branches.

\(r = \dfrac{6}{1 - 2\cos\theta}\)
