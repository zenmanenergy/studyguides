FRQ Time Allocation (Suggested)

FRQ 1 -- The Synthesis Essay

You are given 6-7 sources (articles, data, images, charts) on a single topic. You must write an argument that synthesizes at least 3 sources to support your position.

Scoring DimensionPointsWhat Earns It
Thesis0-1Arguable claim that responds to the prompt; not a restatement
Evidence & Commentary0-4Use of evidence from at least 3 sources with explanation; commentary connecting to thesis
Sophistication0-1Nuance, complexity, compelling argument structure, or vivid style

Synthesis Strategy:

  1. Read all sources quickly during the 15-minute reading period; annotate key claims and evidence
  2. Decide your position on the prompt question
  3. Identify which sources support, complicate, or oppose your view
  4. Write a thesis that reflects your position and previews your reasoning
  5. Use sources to support your points -- not as the point itself. Synthesize means YOU drive the argument; sources are evidence
  6. Do NOT agree with all sources equally -- take a position and use sources selectively
A critical synthesis mistake: writing a "tour of the sources" instead of an argument. Each paragraph should advance YOUR claim using evidence from sources -- not just summarize each source in turn.

FRQ 2 -- The Rhetorical Analysis Essay

You are given one nonfiction prose passage and asked to analyze how the writer uses rhetorical strategies to achieve their purpose.

Scoring DimensionPointsWhat Earns It
Thesis0-1Arguable claim about the writer's rhetorical choices and their effect; not a list of devices
Evidence & Commentary0-4Specific textual evidence with analysis of how/why the choice is effective
Sophistication0-1Nuanced analysis of multiple layers of the text; complex argument about how strategies work together

Rhetorical Analysis Structure:

  1. Read the passage annotating: underline choices (diction, syntax, figurative language); note tone shifts; identify the rhetorical situation
  2. Write a thesis that makes a claim about HOW and WHY -- not just WHAT devices are used
  3. Organize by argument, not by device ("Adams builds credibility through..." not "Adams uses ethos, pathos, and logos...")
  4. Quote specifically; analyze immediately; connect to purpose
  5. Return to the larger rhetorical situation in your conclusion
Weak thesis: "The author uses ethos, pathos, and logos to persuade." Strong thesis: "By grounding her appeal in shared cultural memory and then pivoting to urgent statistical evidence, the author constructs a case that is simultaneously emotionally resonant and intellectually credible."

FRQ 3 -- The Argument Essay

You are given a prompt with a claim or question. You must take and defend a position using evidence from your reading, experience, or observation.

Scoring DimensionPointsWhat Earns It
Thesis0-1Arguable, defensible claim that responds directly to the prompt
Evidence & Commentary0-4Specific evidence (from any source) with explanation of how it proves the claim
Sophistication0-1Nuanced argument; complex reasoning; effective use of style

Argument Essay Strategy:

  1. Spend 5 minutes planning: What is your position? What are your 2-3 strongest arguments? What is the best counterargument?
  2. Write a clear, arguable thesis with a stated line of reasoning
  3. One body paragraph per main argument; lead each with a strong topic sentence
  4. Use specific evidence: a real example, a relevant historical event, a study you know, a literary work
  5. Include one counterargument paragraph with concession and refutation
  6. Conclude by returning to the significance of your thesis

The Sophistication Point

Each essay has one "sophistication" point that rewards complex, nuanced thinking. It is the hardest point to earn. These moves can earn it:

MoveHow to Execute It
Nuanced argumentAcknowledge the limits of your own argument; qualify claims; address genuine complexity
Vivid or precise styleUse strategically effective language choices throughout; not just at the end
Compelling line of reasoningArguments connect logically and build on each other rather than being a disconnected list
Broader implicationsConnect your argument to larger themes, systemic issues, or historical patterns
Explain the "so what"Don't just prove your claim -- explain why it matters and what follows from it

Multiple Choice -- Reading Section Tips

The 45-question multiple choice section includes reading passages and questions about rhetorical analysis and writing effectiveness.

  • For rhetorical analysis questions: always return to the passage; eliminate answers that describe a real device but explain the wrong purpose
  • "Best" answer means most precise and most fully supported -- not just one that is partially correct
  • For "writing" questions (asking what revisions would improve the passage): prioritize clarity, precision, and argumentative coherence
  • Watch for answers that add accurate information but don't address the specific question asked
  • Manage time: aim for 1.5 minutes per question; mark and return to difficult ones
