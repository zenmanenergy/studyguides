AP English Language -- Unit 3: Claims, Reasoning, and Evidence (Reading)

Types of Claims

A claim is the assertion a writer makes that requires support. Recognizing the type of claim tells you what kind of evidence and reasoning to expect.

Claim TypeWhat It AssertsExample
FactSomething is true or false; something happened"Carbon dioxide levels are at their highest in 800,000 years."
DefinitionWhat something means or how it should be categorized"Mass incarceration constitutes a form of systemic racism."
ValueSomething is good, bad, right, or wrong"Universal health care is a moral imperative in a wealthy society."
Cause/EffectOne thing causes or results in another"Social media use causes increased rates of adolescent depression."
PolicySomething should be done"Congress should pass legislation limiting pharmaceutical pricing."
On the AP exam, identifying the claim type helps you evaluate whether the evidence provided is actually sufficient to support it. A policy claim requires different evidence than a factual claim.

Lines of Reasoning

A line of reasoning is the logical structure connecting a claim to its evidence -- the "because" that makes the argument hold together. Strong arguments have multiple lines of reasoning that work together.

Reasoning TypeDefinitionStrength/Weakness
DeductiveGeneral principle applied to a specific case (syllogism)Strong if premise is true; collapses if premise is flawed
InductiveSpecific examples lead to a general conclusionPersuasive with many examples; weak with few (hasty generalization)
AnalogicalArgues that two similar situations should be treated the sameIntuitive; weak if the analogy is imperfect
CausalArgues that one event causes anotherPowerful; must rule out correlation vs. causation
CircumstantialBuilds a case from surrounding evidenceStrong with multiple converging pieces; weak in isolation

Types of Evidence

Writers choose evidence based on their audience's values and the type of claim being made. Recognizing evidence type helps you evaluate its appropriateness and strength.

Evidence TypeDescriptionBest Used For
Statistics and dataQuantified facts from studies, surveys, or official sourcesFactual and causal claims; logos-based arguments
Expert testimonyStatements from credible authorities in a fieldComplex topics where expertise matters; builds ethos
Specific examplesConcrete cases that illustrate the claimMaking abstract claims tangible; building logos and pathos
AnecdoteA brief narrative, often personal or reportedBuilding pathos; humanizing an issue
Historical evidencePast events used as precedent or analogyPolicy claims; showing patterns over time
Hypothetical scenariosImagined situations used to test principlesPhilosophical arguments; thought experiments

Evaluating Evidence Quality

Not all evidence is equally valid. AP passages often present evidence of varying quality, and you need to assess how well it supports the claim.

  • Relevance: Does the evidence actually support THIS claim?
  • Recency: Is the data current enough for the argument?
  • Source credibility: Is the source expert, unbiased, and reputable?
  • Sufficiency: Is there enough evidence to support the scope of the claim?
  • Representativeness: Does the evidence reflect the full picture or only a selective view?
Correlation vs. causation: A statistic showing two things happen together does NOT prove one causes the other. Strong writers acknowledge this; weak arguments conflate the two.

Logical Fallacies

A logical fallacy is an error in reasoning that weakens an argument. Recognizing fallacies is an AP skill both for analyzing texts and avoiding them in your own writing.

FallacyDescriptionExample
Ad HominemAttacking the person rather than the argument"Senator X's climate proposal is worthless; she's a hypocrite."
Straw ManMisrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack"People who support gun control want to leave citizens defenseless."
False DilemmaPresenting only two options when more exist"You're either with us or against us."
Slippery SlopeClaiming a small step inevitably leads to extreme consequences"If we allow this, soon the government will control everything."
Hasty GeneralizationDrawing a broad conclusion from insufficient examples"I met two rude New Yorkers; New Yorkers are rude."
Appeal to AuthorityUsing an expert's statement on an unrelated field"A famous actor says this vaccine is dangerous."
Circular ReasoningThe conclusion is used as evidence for itself"This law is just because it is lawful."
Post HocAssuming causation from sequence (after this, therefore because of this)"I wore my lucky socks and we won; the socks caused the win."

Counterarguments and Concession

Strong arguers acknowledge opposing views. Recognizing how a writer handles counterarguments tells you about their rhetorical strategy and credibility.

  • Acknowledgment: Recognizing that an opposing view exists without engaging it fully
  • Concession: Granting that the opposing view has merit in some respects
  • Refutation: Providing reasoning and evidence that the opposing view is wrong or limited
  • Rebuttal: Directly countering the opposing argument with superior evidence
A writer who ignores counterarguments looks naive. A writer who concedes and then refutes them appears intellectually honest and strengthens their overall credibility.
