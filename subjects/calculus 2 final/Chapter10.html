Topic 1 — Sequence Limits

A sequence converges if \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L\) for some finite \(L\). For rational sequences, divide numerator and denominator by the highest power of \(n\).

Question 1

Find the limit of the following sequence, if it converges. If it diverges, write DIV for your answer. Write the exact answer. Do not round. \[ a_n = \frac{8n + 5}{5n - 6} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Divide numerator and denominator by \(n\).

\[ a_n = \frac{8 + \tfrac{5}{n}}{5 - \tfrac{6}{n}} \]

Step 2 — Take the limit as \(n \to \infty\).

\(\tfrac{5}{n} \to 0\) and \(\tfrac{6}{n} \to 0\), so:

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \frac{8 + 0}{5 - 0} = \frac{8}{5} \]

The sequence converges to \(\dfrac{8}{5}\)

Topic 2 — Recursive Sequences (Generalized Fibonacci)

For a recursive sequence defined by \(a_n = a_{n-1} + a_{n-2}\), compute each term by adding the two preceding terms.

Question 2

Determine the first five terms of the following generalized Fibonacci sequence. Please simplify your solution. \[ a_1 = 5, \quad a_2 = 13, \quad a_n = a_{n-1} + a_{n-2} \text{ for } n \geq 3 \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — List the first two terms.

\(a_1 = 5\), \quad \(a_2 = 13\)

Step 2 — Apply the recurrence.

\(a_3 = a_2 + a_1 = 13 + 5 = 18\)

\(a_4 = a_3 + a_2 = 18 + 13 = 31\)

\(a_5 = a_4 + a_3 = 31 + 18 = 49\)

First five terms: \(5,\ 13,\ 18,\ 31,\ 49\)

Topic 3 — Geometric Series Convergence and Sum

A geometric series \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n\) converges to \(\dfrac{a}{1-r}\) when \(|r| < 1\), and diverges when \(|r| \geq 1\).

Question 3

Determine whether the geometric series converges or diverges. If it converges, find its sum. Write the exact answer. Do not round. \[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 6 \cdot \left(\frac{11}{12}\right)^n \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Identify \(a\) and \(r\).

\(a = 6\), \(r = \dfrac{11}{12}\). Since \(|r| = \dfrac{11}{12} < 1\), the series converges.

Step 2 — Apply the geometric series formula.

\[ S = \frac{a}{1 - r} = \frac{6}{1 - \tfrac{11}{12}} = \frac{6}{\tfrac{1}{12}} = 72 \]

The series converges to \(72\)

Topic 4 — Geometric Series (Rewriting the General Term)

Rewrite the general term to identify the first term \(a\) and ratio \(r\) explicitly, then apply the geometric sum formula.

Question 4

Decide whether the series converges or diverges. If the series converges, find its sum. If the series diverges, explain why. \[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{5^{n-1}}{7^n} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Rewrite to isolate the geometric structure.

\[ \frac{5^{n-1}}{7^n} = \frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{5^n}{7^n} = \frac{1}{5}\left(\frac{5}{7}\right)^n \]

Step 2 — Identify \(a\) and \(r\).

\(a = \dfrac{1}{5}\), \(r = \dfrac{5}{7}\). Since \(|r| < 1\), the series converges.

Step 3 — Compute the sum.

\[ S = \frac{1/5}{1 - 5/7} = \frac{1/5}{2/7} = \frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{7}{2} = \frac{7}{10} \]

The series converges to \(\dfrac{7}{10}\)

Topic 5 — Integral Test

If \(f(x)\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing on \([1, \infty)\), then \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f(n)\) and \(\int_1^\infty f(x)\, dx\) either both converge or both diverge.

Question 5

Use the Integral Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges. \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{14}{n^{3/2}} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Verify conditions.

\(f(x) = 14x^{-3/2}\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing on \([1, \infty)\). Integral Test applies.

Step 2 — Evaluate the improper integral.

\[ \int_1^\infty 14x^{-3/2}\, dx = \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[ 14 \cdot \frac{x^{-1/2}}{-1/2} \right]_1^t = \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[ -\frac{28}{\sqrt{x}} \right]_1^t \]

\(= \lim_{t \to \infty} \left( -\dfrac{28}{\sqrt{t}} + 28 \right) = 0 + 28 = 28\)

Step 3 — Conclude.

The integral converges, so the series converges. (This is also a \(p\)-series with \(p = \tfrac{3}{2} > 1\).)

The series converges

Topic 6 — Direct Comparison Test

If \(0 \leq a_n \leq b_n\) for all \(n\) and \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges. If \(a_n \geq b_n \geq 0\) and \(\sum b_n\) diverges, then \(\sum a_n\) diverges.

Question 6

Use the Direct Comparison Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges. \[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2 + 7^n} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Find a comparison series.

Note that \(2 + 7^n > 7^n\), so \(\dfrac{1}{2 + 7^n} < \dfrac{1}{7^n} = \left(\dfrac{1}{7}\right)^n\).

Step 2 — Evaluate the comparison series.

\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\dfrac{1}{7}\right)^n\) is a geometric series with \(r = \dfrac{1}{7} < 1\), so it converges.

Step 3 — Conclude by Direct Comparison.

Since \(0 < \dfrac{1}{2+7^n} < \left(\dfrac{1}{7}\right)^n\) and the larger series converges, our series converges.

The series converges

Topic 7 — Limit Comparison Test

If \(\lim_{n \to \infty} \dfrac{a_n}{b_n} = L\) with \(0 < L < \infty\), then \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) either both converge or both diverge.

Question 7

Use the Limit Comparison Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges. \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{8n^2}{n^3 + 4} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Choose a comparison series.

The dominant terms give \(\dfrac{8n^2}{n^3} = \dfrac{8}{n}\), so compare to \(b_n = \dfrac{1}{n}\).

Step 2 — Compute the limit.

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{8n^2}{n^3 + 4} \cdot n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{8n^3}{n^3 + 4} = 8 \]

Step 3 — Conclude.

\(L = 8 \in (0, \infty)\). The harmonic series \(\sum \dfrac{1}{n}\) diverges, so our series diverges.

The series diverges

Topic 8 — Ratio Test

Compute \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\). Converges if \(L < 1\), diverges if \(L > 1\), inconclusive if \(L = 1\).

Question 8

Use the Ratio Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges. If the Ratio Test is inconclusive, use another method. \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{3^n}{(n + 6)n} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Write \(a_{n+1}/a_n\).

\[ \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \frac{3^{n+1}}{(n+7)(n+1)} \cdot \frac{(n+6)n}{3^n} = \frac{3 \cdot n(n+6)}{(n+1)(n+7)} \]

Step 2 — Take the limit.

\[ L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3n(n+6)}{(n+1)(n+7)} = 3 \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n^2 + 6n}{n^2 + 8n + 7} = 3 \cdot 1 = 3 \]

Step 3 — Conclude.

\(L = 3 > 1\), so the series diverges by the Ratio Test.

The series diverges

Topic 9 — Root Test

Compute \(L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}\). Converges if \(L < 1\), diverges if \(L > 1\), inconclusive if \(L = 1\).

Question 9

Use the Root Test to determine whether the series converges or diverges. If the Root Test is inconclusive, use another method. \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{11n}{n^2 + 11}\right)^n \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Apply the Root Test.

\[ L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\left(\frac{11n}{n^2 + 11}\right)^n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{11n}{n^2 + 11} \]

Step 2 — Evaluate the limit.

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{11n}{n^2 + 11} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{11/n}{1 + 11/n^2} = \frac{0}{1} = 0 \]

Step 3 — Conclude.

\(L = 0 < 1\), so the series converges by the Root Test.

The series converges

Topic 10 — Alternating Series and Absolute Convergence

An alternating series \(\sum (-1)^n b_n\) (with \(b_n > 0\)) converges if \(b_n\) is decreasing and \(b_n \to 0\). It converges absolutely if \(\sum |a_n|\) converges.

Question 10

Determine whether the series converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges. \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{n+1} \frac{2}{n^2 + 9} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Check absolute convergence.

\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left|(-1)^{n+1} \frac{2}{n^2+9}\right| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{2}{n^2+9}\)

Compare to \(\dfrac{2}{n^2}\): since \(\dfrac{2}{n^2+9} < \dfrac{2}{n^2}\) and \(\sum \dfrac{2}{n^2}\) is a convergent \(p\)-series (\(p=2>1\)), the series of absolute values converges.

Step 2 — Conclude.

Since \(\sum |a_n|\) converges, the series converges absolutely.

The series converges absolutely

Topic 11 — Radius and Interval of Convergence

Use the Ratio Test to find the radius of convergence \(R\): \(R = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\dfrac{a_n}{a_{n+1}}\right|\). Then check each endpoint separately.

Question 11

Determine the radius and interval of convergence for the power series. Be sure to check for convergence at the endpoints. Write the exact answer. Do not round. \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(11x)^n}{n^6} \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Apply the Ratio Test.

\[ \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{(11x)^{n+1}}{(n+1)^6} \cdot \frac{n^6}{(11x)^n}\right| = |11x| \cdot \left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^6 \to |11x| \text{ as } n \to \infty \]

Step 2 — Find the radius of convergence.

The series converges when \(|11x| < 1\), i.e., \(|x| < \dfrac{1}{11}\). So \(R = \dfrac{1}{11}\).

Step 3 — Check endpoint \(x = \dfrac{1}{11}\).

\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(11 \cdot \frac{1}{11})^n}{n^6} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^6}\) — a convergent \(p\)-series (\(p=6>1\)). Converges.

Step 4 — Check endpoint \(x = -\dfrac{1}{11}\).

\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^6}\) — converges absolutely since \(\sum \dfrac{1}{n^6}\) converges. Converges.

Radius of convergence: \(R = \dfrac{1}{11}\). Interval of convergence: \(\left[-\dfrac{1}{11},\ \dfrac{1}{11}\right]\)

Topic 12 — Taylor Series of a Polynomial

For a polynomial \(f(x)\) expanded about \(a\): compute \(f(a), f'(a), f''(a), \ldots\) and write \(f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \dfrac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\).

Question 12

Find the four terms of the Taylor series expansion of the function about \(a = 3\). \[ f(x) = 3x^3 - 4x^2 + 2x - 4 \]

Walkthrough

Step 1 — Compute derivatives and evaluate at \(a = 3\).

\(f(x) = 3x^3 - 4x^2 + 2x - 4 \Rightarrow f(3) = 81 - 36 + 6 - 4 = 47\)

\(f'(x) = 9x^2 - 8x + 2 \Rightarrow f'(3) = 81 - 24 + 2 = 59\)

\(f''(x) = 18x - 8 \Rightarrow f''(3) = 54 - 8 = 46\)

\(f'''(x) = 18 \Rightarrow f'''(3) = 18\)

\(f^{(4)}(x) = 0\) (and all higher derivatives are 0)

Step 2 — Write the Taylor series.

\[ f(x) = \frac{47}{0!}(x-3)^0 + \frac{59}{1!}(x-3)^1 + \frac{46}{2!}(x-3)^2 + \frac{18}{3!}(x-3)^3 \]
\[ f(x) = 47 + 59(x-3) + 23(x-3)^2 + 3(x-3)^3 \]

Four terms: \(47 + 59(x-3) + 23(x-3)^2 + 3(x-3)^3\)
