AP Computer Science Principles -- Unit 7: Global Impact and Exam Prep

Computing Innovations

A computing innovation is any software or hardware that has had a significant effect on individuals or society. This is a central theme throughout AP CSP -- you must be able to analyze innovations using a consistent framework.

For any computing innovation, ask: What problem does it solve? What data does it use or produce? What are the beneficial effects? What are the harmful effects? Who is helped? Who is harmed or left out?

GPS / navigation appsDramatically reduces getting lost; enables ride-sharing; assists emergency servicesCongestion routing through residential streets; privacy tracking; skill atrophy

The Digital Divide

The digital divide describes unequal access to technology and its benefits across populations.

  • Divided by income, geography (urban vs. rural), age, disability, and country
  • Lack of broadband in rural areas limits educational and economic opportunities
  • High-speed internet access and device ownership correlate strongly with academic achievement
  • The divide is not just about hardware -- it includes digital literacy: knowing how to use technology effectively and safely
  • Closing the divide requires infrastructure investment, affordable devices, and education

Intellectual Property and Licensing

Digital content raises new challenges for intellectual property law.

TermMeaning
CopyrightAutomatic legal protection of original creative works; others cannot copy without permission
Creative CommonsFlexible licensing that lets creators specify how their work may be used (free use, attribution required, non-commercial only, etc.)
Open sourceSource code is publicly available; others can use, modify, and distribute it under defined license terms
Fair useLimited use of copyrighted material without permission for purposes like education, commentary, parody
PlagiarismPresenting someone else's work as your own; an ethical violation even when not illegal

Computing and Collaboration

Modern software is almost never built by one person. Collaboration introduces benefits and challenges.

  • Version control (e.g., Git): tracks changes to code over time; allows multiple contributors to work simultaneously; enables rollback to earlier versions
  • Pair programming: two programmers work together at one machine; one types, one reviews in real time
  • Diverse teams produce better software -- different perspectives catch different problems and serve broader users
  • Documentation and comments make code understandable to others (and to your future self)

The Create Performance Task

The Create Task requires you to design and implement a program that solves a problem, pursues a personal interest, or creates something. You submit the program and written responses before the exam.

RequirementDetails
Program purpose and functionDescribe what the program does and demonstrate it running
Data abstractionMust use a list (or other collection) to manage complexity; explain how it stores data
Managing complexityShow how the list/collection makes the program more manageable
Procedural abstractionMust define and call a procedure with a parameter that affects its behavior
AlgorithmMust include sequencing, selection, AND iteration within the procedure
TestingDescribe two test cases: one that produces the expected result, one that doesn't -- and explain how you fixed it
The Create Task is scored on whether your written responses accurately describe your code. Write responses that match your actual program. Common error: describing an algorithm or list that is not present in the submitted code.

Multiple Choice Topics to Review

  • Binary / number bases / data size calculations
  • Lossless vs. lossy compression trade-offs
  • Reading and tracing AP pseudocode (especially procedures, lists, and loops)
  • Internet protocols: TCP vs. UDP, HTTP vs. HTTPS, DNS, IP addresses
  • Packet switching and fault tolerance
  • Algorithm efficiency: linear vs. binary search; reasonable vs. unreasonable time
  • The Halting Problem and undecidable problems
  • Symmetric vs. asymmetric encryption; how HTTPS protects data
  • Privacy, data collection, bias, and unintended effects of innovations
  • Beneficial and harmful effects of computing innovations
  • Digital divide and open vs. proprietary standards
  • Intellectual property: copyright, Creative Commons, open source, fair use
