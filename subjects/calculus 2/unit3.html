Calculus 2 — Unit 3: Parametric Equations and Polar Coordinates

9.1 Parametric Equations

Instead of expressing \(y\) directly as a function of \(x\), a parametric curve expresses both \(x\) and \(y\) as functions of a third variable \(t\) (the parameter):

\[ x = f(t), \quad y = g(t), \quad t \in [a, b] \]
  • The curve is traced in the direction of increasing \(t\)
  • Eliminating the parameter: solve one equation for \(t\) and substitute into the other to get \(y = h(x)\). Only valid when the relationship is one-to-one in \(t\).
  • Parametric form can represent curves that fail the vertical line test (e.g., circles)

Common parametric curves:

CurveParametric Form
Circle, radius \(r\), centered at origin\(x = r\cos t,\; y = r\sin t,\; t \in [0, 2\pi]\)
Ellipse \(\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1\)\(x = a\cos t,\; y = b\sin t\)
Line through \((x_0,y_0)\) with slope \(m\)\(x = x_0 + t,\; y = y_0 + mt\)
Cycloid\(x = r(t - \sin t),\; y = r(1 - \cos t)\)

9.2 Calculus and Parametric Equations

First derivative (slope of the tangent line):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} \qquad \left(\frac{dx}{dt} \ne 0\right) \]

Second derivative:

\[ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = \frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}\!\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)}{dx/dt} \]
  • Tangent is horizontal when \(dy/dt = 0\) (and \(dx/dt \ne 0\))
  • Tangent is vertical when \(dx/dt = 0\) (and \(dy/dt \ne 0\))
  • At a point where both are zero: further analysis needed (cusp or self-intersection)

Arc Length of a parametric curve:

\[ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\, dt \]

Area enclosed by a parametric curve (traced once, counter-clockwise):

\[ A = \int_a^b y\, \frac{dx}{dt}\, dt \qquad \text{or} \qquad A = -\int_a^b y\, \frac{dx}{dt}\, dt \text{ (clockwise)} \]

Surface Area of Revolution about the \(x\)-axis:

\[ S = 2\pi \int_a^b y\, \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\, dt \qquad (y \ge 0) \]

9.3 Polar Coordinates

A point \(P\) is described by \((r, \theta)\): distance \(r\) from the origin (pole) and angle \(\theta\) from the polar axis (positive \(x\)-axis).

\[ x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta, \quad r^2 = x^2 + y^2, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x} \]
  • Negative \(r\): the point is in the opposite direction at distance \(|r|\)
  • Each point has infinitely many representations: \((r, \theta) = (r, \theta + 2\pi k) = (-r, \theta + \pi)\)

Common polar curves:

EquationCurve
\(r = a\)Circle, radius \(a\), centered at origin
\(r = a\cos\theta\)Circle, diameter \(a\), tangent to \(y\)-axis at origin
\(r = a\sin\theta\)Circle, diameter \(a\), tangent to \(x\)-axis at origin
\(r = a + b\cos\theta\)Limacon (cardioid if \(a = b\))
\(r = a\cos(n\theta)\)Rose with \(n\) petals (\(n\) odd) or \(2n\) petals (\(n\) even)
\(r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)\)Lemniscate of Bernoulli
\(r = a\theta\)Archimedean spiral

9.4 Calculus in Polar Coordinates

Slope of tangent line (using parametric with \(t = \theta\)):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta} = \frac{r'(\theta)\sin\theta + r(\theta)\cos\theta}{r'(\theta)\cos\theta - r(\theta)\sin\theta} \]

Area enclosed by \(r = f(\theta)\) on \([\alpha, \beta]\):

\[ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta [f(\theta)]^2\, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\, d\theta \]

Area between two polar curves \(r_1 \ge r_2\):

\[ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \left([r_1(\theta)]^2 - [r_2(\theta)]^2\right) d\theta \]

Arc Length in polar:

\[ L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}\, d\theta \]
  • To find intersection points of two polar curves, set \(r_1(\theta) = r_2(\theta)\) AND check the pole \((r=0)\) separately -- curves may pass through the pole at different angles

9.5 Conic Sections in Cartesian Coordinates

ConicStandard FormKey Features
Parabola\(y^2 = 4px\) (opens right) or \(x^2 = 4py\) (opens up)Focus at \((p,0)\) or \((0,p)\); directrix \(x = -p\) or \(y = -p\)
Ellipse\(\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1,\; a > b > 0\)Foci at \((\pm c, 0)\), \(c^2 = a^2 - b^2\); eccentricity \(e = c/a < 1\)
Hyperbola\(\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1\)Foci at \((\pm c, 0)\), \(c^2 = a^2 + b^2\); asymptotes \(y = \pm\frac{b}{a}x\); \(e = c/a > 1\)
  • Eccentricity: \(e = 0\) (circle), \(0 < e < 1\) (ellipse), \(e = 1\) (parabola), \(e > 1\) (hyperbola)
  • Completing the square converts general form \(Ax^2 + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0\) to standard form
  • Reflective property of parabola: rays parallel to the axis reflect through the focus
  • Ellipse: sum of distances from any point to both foci = \(2a\); used in planetary orbits (Kepler's first law)
  • Hyperbola: difference of distances from any point to both foci = \(2a\)

9.6 Conic Sections in Polar Coordinates

All three conic sections can be written in a unified polar form with focus at the origin:

\[ r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\cos\theta} \qquad \text{or} \qquad r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\sin\theta} \]
  • \(e\) = eccentricity, \(d\) = distance from focus to directrix
  • \(+\cos\theta\): directrix to the right; \(-\cos\theta\): directrix to the left
  • \(+\sin\theta\): directrix above; \(-\sin\theta\): directrix below
EccentricityConic
\(e = 1\)Parabola
\(0 < e < 1\)Ellipse
\(e > 1\)Hyperbola
  • The semi-latus rectum is \(\ell = ed\); it is the \(r\)-value when \(\theta = \pi/2\)
  • Kepler's first law: planetary orbits are ellipses with the Sun at one focus -- expressed naturally in polar conic form
