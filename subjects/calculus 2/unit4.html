Calculus 2 — Unit 4: Sequences and Series

10.1 Sequences

A sequence \(\{a_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is an ordered list of numbers. The key question is whether the sequence converges (approaches a finite limit) or diverges.

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{sequence converges to } L \]
  • If the limit DNE or is \(\pm\infty\), the sequence diverges
  • Limit laws, Squeeze Theorem, and L'Hopital's Rule (treating \(n\) as a continuous variable) all apply
  • \(\lim_{n\to\infty} r^n = 0\) if \(|r| < 1\); diverges if \(|r| \ge 1\) (except \(r=1\) gives 1)
  • \(\lim_{n\to\infty} n^{1/n} = 1\), \quad \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\ln n}{n} = 0\)

Monotone Convergence Theorem: A sequence that is bounded AND monotone (always increasing or always decreasing) must converge.

TypeCondition
Increasing\(a_{n+1} \ge a_n\) for all \(n\)
Decreasing\(a_{n+1} \le a_n\) for all \(n\)
Bounded above\(a_n \le M\) for all \(n\)
Bounded below\(a_n \ge m\) for all \(n\)

10.2 Infinite Series

An infinite series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) is the limit of the sequence of partial sums \(S_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n\).

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n = \lim_{N\to\infty} S_N \]

Geometric Series (most important series to know):

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r} \qquad |r| < 1 \quad \text{(diverges if } |r| \ge 1\text{)} \]

Divergence Test (Test for Divergence):

\[ \text{If } \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n \ne 0, \text{ then } \sum a_n \text{ diverges.} \]
  • Warning: if \(\lim a_n = 0\), the series may still diverge (e.g., harmonic series \(\sum 1/n\))

Telescoping Series: Most terms cancel when \(S_N\) is expanded. Find a partial fraction decomposition.

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n(n+1)} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{n} - \frac{1}{n+1}\right) = 1 \]

10.3 The Integral Test

If \(f\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing on \([1, \infty)\) with \(f(n) = a_n\), then:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \text{ converges} \iff \int_1^\infty f(x)\, dx \text{ converges} \]

p-Series:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p} \begin{cases} \text{converges} & p > 1 \\ \text{diverges} & p \le 1 \end{cases} \]
  • The harmonic series \(\sum 1/n\) diverges (\(p = 1\))
  • The Integral Test gives remainder estimates: \(R_N = S - S_N \le \int_N^\infty f(x)\,dx\)

10.4 Comparison Tests

Direct Comparison Test: For series with positive terms, if \(0 \le a_n \le b_n\) for all sufficiently large \(n\):

  • If \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges
  • If \(\sum a_n\) diverges, then \(\sum b_n\) diverges

Limit Comparison Test: If \(a_n, b_n > 0\) and

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = c \qquad (0 < c < \infty) \]

then \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) either both converge or both diverge.

  • If \(c = 0\): convergence of \(\sum b_n\) implies convergence of \(\sum a_n\)
  • If \(c = \infty\): divergence of \(\sum b_n\) implies divergence of \(\sum a_n\)
  • Strategy: compare with a geometric series or p-series that behaves like \(a_n\) for large \(n\)

10.5 The Ratio and Root Tests

Ratio Test:

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| \]
Value of \(L\)Conclusion
\(L < 1\)Series converges absolutely
\(L > 1\) (or \(L = \infty\))Series diverges
\(L = 1\)Inconclusive -- use another test

Root Test:

\[ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = \lim_{n\to\infty} |a_n|^{1/n} \]
  • Same conclusions as the Ratio Test
  • Root Test is most useful when \(a_n = [f(n)]^n\) (something to the \(n\)th power)
  • Ratio Test is most useful for factorials and products: \(n!\), \(r^n\), or products

10.6 Absolute and Conditional Convergence

A series \(\sum a_n\) is absolutely convergent if \(\sum |a_n|\) converges. Absolute convergence implies convergence.

A series is conditionally convergent if \(\sum a_n\) converges but \(\sum |a_n|\) diverges.

Alternating Series Test (Leibniz's Test): \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n-1} b_n\) converges if:

  • \(b_n > 0\) for all \(n\)
  • \(b_{n+1} \le b_n\) (terms are decreasing)
  • \(\lim_{n\to\infty} b_n = 0\)

Alternating Series Estimation Theorem:

\[ |S - S_N| \le b_{N+1} \qquad \text{(error is bounded by the first omitted term)} \]

Classic example: the alternating harmonic series \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} = \ln 2\) converges conditionally.

10.7 Power Series

A power series centered at \(a\) is:

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-a) + c_2(x-a)^2 + \cdots \]

It converges for \(|x - a| < R\) where \(R\) is the radius of convergence. The interval of convergence is \((a-R,\, a+R)\) with endpoints checked separately.

\[ R = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} \left|\frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right|} \qquad \text{(from the Ratio Test)} \]
  • \(R = 0\): series only converges at \(x = a\)
  • \(R = \infty\): series converges for all \(x\)

Term-by-term differentiation and integration: Within the interval of convergence:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n\, c_n(x-a)^{n-1}, \qquad \int \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n\, dx = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{c_n}{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1} + C \]
  • The radius of convergence is preserved under differentiation and integration; endpoints may differ

10.8 Taylor and Maclaurin Series

The Taylor series of \(f\) centered at \(a\):

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2 + \cdots \]

A Maclaurin series is a Taylor series centered at \(a = 0\).

Essential Maclaurin series to memorize:

FunctionSeriesInterval of Convergence
\(e^x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots\)\((-\infty, \infty)\)
\(\sin x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = x - \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^5}{120} - \cdots\)\((-\infty, \infty)\)
\(\cos x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^4}{24} - \cdots\)\((-\infty, \infty)\)
\(\dfrac{1}{1-x}\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \cdots\)\((-1, 1)\)
\(\ln(1+x)\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1} x^n}{n} = x - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} - \cdots\)\((-1, 1]\)
\(\arctan x\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{2n+1} = x - \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{x^5}{5} - \cdots\)\([-1, 1]\)
\((1+x)^k\)\(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{k}{n} x^n\) (binomial series)\((-1, 1)\) (or larger, depends on \(k\))

Taylor's Remainder Theorem: The error in using the degree-\(n\) Taylor polynomial \(T_n(x)\):

\[ |R_n(x)| = |f(x) - T_n(x)| \le \frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x - a|^{n+1} \]

where \(M\) is a bound on \(|f^{(n+1)}|\) between \(x\) and \(a\).

10.9 Further Applications of Series

Approximating definite integrals: When an antiderivative has no closed form (e.g., \(\int e^{-x^2}\, dx\)), substitute the Maclaurin series and integrate term by term:

\[ \int_0^1 e^{-x^2}\, dx = \int_0^1 \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{n!}\, dx = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n!(2n+1)} \]

Euler's Formula and complex exponentials:

\[ e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x, \qquad e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 \]

Binomial Series:

\[ (1+x)^k = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{k}{n} x^n, \qquad \binom{k}{n} = \frac{k(k-1)(k-2)\cdots(k-n+1)}{n!} \]
  • When \(k\) is a non-negative integer, this terminates to the familiar Binomial Theorem
  • For non-integer \(k\) and \(|x| < 1\), gives an infinite series for functions like \(\sqrt{1+x}\) or \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\)

Multiplication and composition of series: Known series can be combined to find new ones. Example: \(e^x \sin x\) by multiplying the two Maclaurin series term by term.
