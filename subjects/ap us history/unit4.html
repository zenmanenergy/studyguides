AP US History — Unit 4: Period 4 (1800–1848)

Jeffersonian Democracy and Expansion

  • Louisiana Purchase (1803): doubled US size; Napoleon sold it; Jefferson set aside strict constitutionalism
  • Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–06): mapped West; charted relations with Native tribes
  • Marbury v. Madison (1803): established judicial review; Marshall Court expanded federal power
  • Embargo Act (1807): banned trade with Britain/France; backfired — hurt US economy, especially New England
  • War of 1812: vs. Britain; ended in stalemate (Treaty of Ghent); boosted American nationalism

Market Revolution and Economic Change

  • Shift from subsistence farming to market-oriented production; wage labor grew
  • Erie Canal (1825): linked Great Lakes to NYC; transformed internal commerce
  • Lowell System: factory system using young women ("Lowell girls"); early industrialization in New England
  • Eli Whitney's interchangeable parts; rise of manufacturing in Northeast
  • Economic sectionalism deepened: North = industry; South = cotton/slavery; West = farming
  • Cotton gin (1793) dramatically increased cotton production and demand for enslaved labor

Jacksonian Democracy

  • Andrew Jackson (1829–37): "common man" democracy; expanded white male suffrage; spoils system; states' rights
  • Nullification Crisis (1832): South Carolina nullified tariff; Jackson threatened force; compromise reached
  • Bank War: Jackson vetoed recharter of Second Bank of US; destroyed it; caused economic instability
  • Indian Removal Act (1830): forced removal of Five Civilized Tribes; Cherokee's Trail of Tears (1838–39) killed ~4,000
  • Worcester v. Georgia (1832): Supreme Court ruled for Cherokee; Jackson reportedly ignored it

Manifest Destiny (early)

  • Belief that American expansion across North America was divinely ordained and inevitable
  • Texas independence from Mexico (1836); Texas annexation debate divided Congress over slavery expansion
  • Oregon Trail; settlers moved westward in large numbers; "54-40 or Fight" vs. Britain over Oregon (settled at 49th parallel, 1846)
  • Displacement and conflict with Native peoples accelerated as settlers pushed west
