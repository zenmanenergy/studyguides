AP US History — Unit 6: Period 6 (1865–1898)

Industrialization and the Gilded Age

  • Transcontinental Railroad completed 1869; connected markets, enabled settlement, relied on Chinese immigrant labor
  • Captains of Industry / Robber Barons: Andrew Carnegie (steel), John D. Rockefeller (oil/Standard Oil), J.P. Morgan (banking)
  • Vertical integration: Carnegie controlled every step of steel production
  • Horizontal integration / monopolies: Rockefeller used trusts to dominate entire industries
  • Social Darwinism: Herbert Spencer argued the wealthy "deserved" their position; used to oppose regulation
  • Gospel of Wealth: Carnegie argued wealthy had a duty to use their money for public good

Immigration and Urbanization

  • "Old Immigration" (pre-1880): Northwest Europe — Irish, German, Scandinavian
  • "New Immigration" (1880–1920): Southern and Eastern Europe — Italians, Poles, Jews, Russians; also Chinese (West Coast)
  • Immigrants settled in ethnic urban neighborhoods; supplied cheap industrial labor
  • Chinese Exclusion Act (1882): first major federal immigration restriction based on ethnicity
  • Jane Addams founded Hull House (1889) in Chicago: settlement house providing services to immigrant poor
  • Nativist backlash: American Protective Association; fear of Catholic and Jewish immigrants

The New South and Racial Violence

  • Jim Crow laws: segregation codified across the South after Reconstruction ended
  • Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): Supreme Court upheld "separate but equal"; legalized segregation for 60 years
  • Ida B. Wells: journalist; documented and campaigned against lynching
  • Booker T. Washington: accommodation strategy; vocational education; Atlanta Compromise (1895)
  • W.E.B. Du Bois: challenged Washington; demanded full civil rights; "the talented tenth"; later co-founded NAACP (1909)

Farmers and the Populist Movement

  • Farmers squeezed by falling crop prices, railroad monopolies, and debt (deflation hurt debtors)
  • Grange movement → Farmers' Alliances → People's Party (Populists)
  • Populist platform (1892): graduated income tax, direct election of senators, regulation of railroads, silver coinage
  • William Jennings Bryan: "Cross of Gold" speech (1896); Democrats adopted silver platform; lost to McKinley
  • Populism faded after 1896 but many demands were later enacted (16th, 17th Amendments)
