AP US History — Unit 3: Period 3 (1754–1800)

French and Indian War (1754–1763)

  • Britain vs. France (with Native allies) for control of Ohio River Valley; part of global Seven Years' War
  • George Washington's early defeat at Fort Necessity (1754) sparked the war
  • Britain won; Treaty of Paris (1763): France ceded Canada and land east of Mississippi to Britain
  • Pontiac's Rebellion (1763): Native resistance to British expansion; led to Proclamation of 1763 (no settlement west of Appalachians)
  • War left Britain deeply in debt → decision to tax colonies → road to Revolution

Revolutionary War and Independence

  • Common Sense (1776): Thomas Paine argued for independence in plain language; mass audience
  • Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776): Jefferson's Lockean natural rights — life, liberty, pursuit of happiness
  • Key battles: Bunker Hill (1775), Trenton (1776), Saratoga (1777, turning point), Yorktown (1781)
  • Saratoga convinced France to ally with colonists (key to victory)
  • Treaty of Paris (1783): Britain recognized US independence; land to Mississippi River
  • Women, enslaved people, Natives largely excluded from "all men are created equal"

Articles of Confederation and the Constitution

  • Articles of Confederation (1781): weak central government; no power to tax or regulate commerce; no executive
  • Shays' Rebellion (1786): debt-ridden farmers revolted; showed weakness of Articles; prompted Constitutional Convention
  • Constitutional Convention (1787): Great Compromise (bicameral Congress); 3/5 Compromise; Electoral College
  • Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay) vs. Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry) debated ratification; Bill of Rights added 1791
  • Federalist Papers: #10 (factions), #51 (separation of powers) — most AP-tested

The Early Republic (1789–1800)

  • Washington: set precedents — two-term limit, cabinet, Farewell Address warned against permanent alliances and parties
  • Hamilton's Financial Plan: national bank, assumption of state debts, tariffs; Jefferson and Madison opposed → birth of party system
  • Federalists (Hamilton): strong central government, commercial economy, pro-British
  • Democratic-Republicans (Jefferson): states' rights, agrarian ideal, pro-French
  • Alien and Sedition Acts (1798): targeted immigrants and critics; Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions (nullification theory) in response
  • Election of 1800: "Revolution of 1800" — peaceful transfer of power from Federalists to Republicans
