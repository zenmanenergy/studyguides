AP English Language -- Unit 2: Rhetorical Situation (Writing)

Writing With Rhetorical Awareness

In Unit 2, you become the writer. A rhetorically aware writer does not simply "write an essay" -- they make deliberate choices about how to communicate based on who they are writing for, why, and in what context.

Every writing choice you make -- your word selection, sentence length, use of evidence, tone -- should be driven by a clear sense of your rhetorical situation.
Writing DecisionDriven by Rhetorical Situation Element
Formal vs. informal vocabularyAudience and context
Amount of background providedAudience knowledge level
Types of evidence usedAudience values and purpose
Degree of urgency in toneExigence and purpose
Length and structureGenre, medium, and occasion

Knowing Your Audience

Audience analysis is the most important step before writing. Writers adapt their choices to what their audience already believes, values, and knows.

  • Primary audience: the main intended readers
  • Secondary audience: others who may read the text (policymakers reading a newspaper column, for example)
  • Sympathetic audience: already agrees with your position; needs reinforcement and calls to action
  • Hostile audience: disagrees; needs concessions, credibility, and carefully framed evidence
  • Neutral audience: undecided; needs clear reasoning and balanced evidence
Adapting to a hostile audience does NOT mean abandoning your argument. It means opening with common ground, acknowledging valid concerns, and using ethos-building strategies before presenting your strongest claims.

Genre and Medium

The genre is the type of writing (editorial, letter, speech, personal essay, report), and the medium is how it is delivered (print, online, spoken). Both affect your choices.

Establishing Purpose and Thesis

Your purpose should be clear and specific -- not just "to argue about social media" but "to argue that teenagers' access to social media should be regulated to protect mental health." The thesis is the written expression of that purpose.

  • A strong thesis is arguable (not just a fact), specific, and significant
  • It previews the logic of your argument, not just your position
  • On the AP exam: a thesis earns 1 point; it must do more than restate the prompt
AP Argument Thesis Formula: [Claim] because/although/while [reasoning/nuance]. Example: "While social media enables global connection, its algorithmic design actively promotes anxiety in adolescents and must be subject to regulatory oversight."

Writer's Persona and Credibility

Even when writing a structured essay, you project a persona -- a version of yourself shaped by tone, word choice, and how you handle evidence. A trustworthy persona builds ethos.

  • Acknowledge complexity and uncertainty where it exists -- it shows intellectual honesty
  • Avoid absolutist language ("always," "everyone knows") unless fully supported
  • Use hedges appropriately: "evidence suggests," "it is likely that"
  • Cite sources accurately; misrepresenting evidence destroys credibility
  • Match your persona to context: conversational for personal essay, authoritative for policy argument

Introductions and Conclusions

Your introduction and conclusion frame your entire argument. They create and fulfill the rhetorical contract with your reader.

Anecdote or scenarioCreates immediate engagement; builds pathos
