Early Settlement: Jamestown and the Chesapeake

  • Jamestown (1607): first permanent English settlement; near collapse until tobacco saved the colony
  • John Rolfe: introduced tobacco cultivation; married Pocahontas (1614)
  • Indentured servitude: white servants worked 4–7 years for passage; freed servants created restless underclass
  • Bacon's Rebellion (1676): frontier farmers vs. Governor Berkeley; exposed class tensions; accelerated shift to African slavery
  • House of Burgesses (1619): first representative assembly in British America

New England: Puritans and Religion

  • Pilgrims: Separatists at Plymouth (1620); Mayflower Compact = self-governance
  • Puritans: Massachusetts Bay Colony (1630); "city upon a hill" (John Winthrop)
  • Roger Williams: banished; founded Rhode Island with religious tolerance and separation of church/state
  • Anne Hutchinson: antinomianism trial; banished 1638 — challenged Puritan authority and gender norms
  • King Philip's War (1675–76): deadliest colonial conflict; Wampanoag vs. colonists; Natives defeated, tribes destroyed
  • Salem Witch Trials (1692): social anxiety, factionalism, misogyny; 20 executed

Slavery and the Atlantic World

  • Transatlantic slave trade: 12+ million Africans forcibly transported, ~2 million died crossing (Middle Passage)
  • Triangular Trade: manufactured goods to Africa → enslaved people to Americas → raw materials to Europe
  • Virginia's slave codes (1705) legally defined slavery as race-based and hereditary
  • Stono Rebellion (1739, South Carolina): largest slave revolt in colonial era; led to harsher slave codes
  • By 1750: enslaved Africans outnumbered whites in South Carolina

Colonial Society and Governance

  • Salutary neglect: Britain loosely enforced colonial laws; colonies developed self-rule habits
  • Each colony had a governor (often appointed by Crown) and an elected assembly
  • Enlightenment influence: John Locke's natural rights; reason over tradition; challenged inherited authority
  • Great Awakening (1730s–40s): evangelical religious revival; preachers like Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield; undermined established churches; spread democratic ideas
  • Print culture: Ben Franklin's Pennsylvania Gazette; John Peter Zenger trial (1735) established precedent for freedom of the press
