AP English Language -- Unit 7: Style (Reading)

What Is Style?

Style refers to the distinctive way a writer uses language to achieve their rhetorical purpose. Style is not decoration -- it is argument. Every stylistic choice either supports or undermines what the writer is trying to accomplish.

When analyzing style on the AP exam, never stop at identification. Always explain: HOW does this stylistic choice work? WHAT effect does it have on the reader? WHY did the writer choose it for THIS audience in THIS context?

Style operates across several dimensions:

  • Diction: word choice and level of language
  • Syntax: sentence structure and arrangement
  • Tone: the attitude conveyed through language
  • Figurative language: non-literal language for effect
  • Imagery: sensory language that creates mental pictures
  • Voice: the distinctive personality of the writing

Diction -- Word Choice

Diction is the single most powerful component of style. The words a writer selects carry connotation, tone, and argument all at once.

Diction ConceptDefinitionExample
DenotationThe literal, dictionary meaning of a word"Home" = a place where one lives
ConnotationThe emotional, cultural, or implied meanings of a word"Home" connotes warmth, belonging, safety
Formal dictionAcademic or elevated vocabulary; distanced, authoritative"The phenomenon precipitates significant consequences."
Informal/colloquial dictionEveryday language; creates intimacy or accessibility"This whole mess started when..."
Loaded languageWords with strong positive or negative connotations used strategically"terrorist" vs. "freedom fighter"
JargonTechnical vocabulary of a specific field; establishes expertise or excludes outsidersLegal, medical, or academic terminology
EuphemismGentle or indirect language for something harsh or taboo"Passed away" instead of "died"

Syntax -- Sentence Structure

How a writer constructs sentences creates rhythm, emphasis, and emotional effect. Sentence variety is a mark of skilled writing.

Syntax FeatureDefinition and Effect
Periodic sentenceMain clause comes at the end; creates suspense and builds emphasis: "Despite the rain, the distance, and every obstacle put before them, they arrived."
Cumulative sentenceMain clause comes first, then modifiers; creates an accumulating, flowing effect
Short sentencesCreate impact, urgency, finality; good for emphasis after longer passages
Parallel structureRepeating grammatical patterns for rhythm and memorability: "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds..."
AnaphoraRepeating a word/phrase at the start of successive clauses: "I have a dream that..." -- creates emphasis and emotional power
AsyndetonOmitting conjunctions for fast pace: "He came, he saw, he conquered."
PolysyndetonExcessive conjunctions for a slow, accumulating effect: "...and the wind and the rain and the cold..."
InversionUnusual word order for emphasis: "Rarely does one encounter such honesty."

Figurative Language

Figurative language makes abstract ideas concrete, creates emotional resonance, and reveals a writer's perspective through the comparisons they choose.

DeviceDefinitionRhetorical Use
SimileComparison using "like" or "as"Makes abstract ideas accessible; creates vivid imagery
MetaphorDirect comparison without "like" or "as"Asserts identity between two things; shapes how the reader conceptualizes the subject
Extended metaphorA single metaphor developed across a passageCreates coherent framework for understanding a complex idea
PersonificationGiving human qualities to non-human thingsCreates emotional connection; makes abstract forces feel immediate
AllusionReference to a well-known text, person, or eventCreates layers of meaning; assumes shared cultural knowledge with audience
HyperboleExtreme exaggeration for effectCreates emphasis or humor; can indicate frustration or passion
UnderstatementRepresenting something as less significant than it isCreates irony; can be more powerful than overstatement
OxymoronJuxtaposing contradictory termsCaptures paradox; forces reader to hold two ideas in tension

Imagery and Sensory Language

Imagery refers to language that appeals to the senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell). Vivid imagery creates emotional immediacy and makes abstract arguments feel real.

  • Visual imagery is most common, but the most powerful writers use multiple senses
  • Imagery creates pathos by putting the reader inside an experience
  • The choice of what to make vivid -- and what to leave vague -- reveals what the writer values
  • Imagery in argument: a statistic about poverty is less persuasive than a carefully described scene of a family without heat in winter

Voice and Tone Revisited

Voice is the distinctive personality that emerges from a writer's combination of style choices. Tone is the emotional attitude expressed -- and it can shift.

  • Voice is consistent across a work; tone can shift from paragraph to paragraph
  • A writer can have a warm, conversational voice while adopting an urgent or indignant tone on a specific issue
  • Tonal shifts are often significant: note where they occur and why
AP exam vocabulary for tone: sardonic, elegiac, wistful, fervent, measured, conciliatory, incisive, nostalgic, indignant, reverent, skeptical, ironic, contemplative, didactic, plaintive.
