AP English Language -- Unit 1: Rhetorical Situation (Reading)

What Is the Rhetorical Situation?

Every text is created within a rhetorical situation -- a specific set of circumstances that shapes how and why a writer communicates. When you read any text on the AP exam, your first task is to identify that situation.

The rhetorical situation includes: exigence (the reason to write), writer/speaker, audience, purpose, subject, and context.
ElementDefinitionQuestion to Ask
ExigenceThe problem, issue, or event that prompts communicationWhy does this need to be said right now?
Speaker/WriterWho is producing the text; their identity, credibility, perspectiveWho is speaking, and what shapes their viewpoint?
AudienceWho the text is intended to reach; their values, knowledge, and relationship to the writerWho is this written for, and what do they already believe?
PurposeWhat the writer wants to achieve -- inform, persuade, entertain, inspireWhat does the writer want the audience to think, feel, or do?
SubjectThe topic being addressedWhat is this text about?
ContextHistorical, cultural, social circumstances surrounding the textWhat is happening in the world that shapes this message?

SOAPS -- A Reading Framework

SOAPS is a tool for quickly analyzing any text's rhetorical situation. Use it when approaching an AP passage.

LetterStands ForWhat to Look For
SSubjectThe literal and implied topic; what the text is about on the surface and beneath
OOccasionThe time and place; the event or circumstance that gave rise to the text
AAudienceThe intended readers/listeners; their background, values, and relationship to the speaker
PPurposeWhat the writer wants to accomplish: persuade, inform, entertain, provoke, memorialize
SSpeakerThe writer's identity, persona, and credibility (ethos); note speaker vs. author distinction
Tip: The speaker and the author are not always the same. A novelist writing in first person is the author; the narrator is the speaker. On the AP exam, distinguish between the two when relevant.

Rhetorical Appeals -- Ethos, Pathos, Logos

Writers use three classical rhetorical appeals to persuade their audiences. Recognizing how they are used -- and how effectively -- is a core AP skill.

AppealDefinitionExamples in Texts
EthosAppeal to credibility, character, or trustworthinessCiting credentials, shared values with audience, consistent tone, reputation
PathosAppeal to emotion, values, or imaginationVivid anecdotes, emotional language, imagery, personal stories
LogosAppeal to logic, reason, and evidenceStatistics, factual examples, expert testimony, logical argument structure
On the AP exam you are not just asked to identify appeals -- you must explain HOW they work and WHY the writer chose them for THIS particular audience in THIS particular context.

Typical Appeal Emphasis by Genre

Kairos -- The Importance of Timing

Kairos refers to the opportune moment -- the right time and place for a message. A speech delivered at the wrong moment loses its power; the same words at the right time can change history.

  • Lincoln's Gettysburg Address was effective partly because of when it was delivered -- amid a brutal war, at a cemetery dedication
  • A letter about climate policy lands differently in the aftermath of a hurricane than on a calm day in winter
  • Recognizing kairos helps explain why a writer emphasizes urgency or uses current events as evidence

Context -- Situational, Cultural, and Historical

Context shapes how a message is received. AP texts often require you to consider the broader circumstances behind a text.

Type of ContextDescription
SituationalThe immediate circumstances: where was the text published, to whom, and when?
HistoricalEvents in history that inform the text's meaning (wars, political movements, social change)
CulturalShared values, norms, and beliefs of the society from which the text emerges
IntertextualHow this text relates to or responds to other texts, traditions, or ideas

Analyzing Tone and Stance

A speaker's tone -- their attitude toward subject and audience -- is a critical part of the rhetorical situation. Tone is revealed through diction, syntax, and figurative language.

  • Stance: the writer's overall position or perspective on the subject
  • Tone: the emotional quality or attitude conveyed in the writing (e.g., earnest, ironic, indignant, reverent)
  • Tone can shift across a text as the writer responds to different parts of the rhetorical situation
  • Multiple tones can coexist: a writer can be both admiring and critical in the same essay
Common tone descriptors for AP: sardonic, elegiac, wistful, urgent, contemplative, didactic, conciliatory, provocative, reverent, skeptical.
